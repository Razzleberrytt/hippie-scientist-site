# Mitragynine Pseudoindoxyl: Chemistry, Pharmacology, and Emerging Risks

> **Strong disclaimer:** This page is educational harm-reduction content only. It is not medical advice, legal advice, dosing guidance, sourcing guidance, or an endorsement of use. Mitragynine pseudoindoxyl is a potent opioid-acting compound with no established safe human dose.

## Evidence Snapshot

Mitragynine pseudoindoxyl is a rearrangement product related to [7-hydroxymitragynine](/compounds/7-hydroxymitragynine). It has shown very high mu-opioid receptor activity in laboratory systems and has been described as a transformation product of 7-hydroxymitragynine in human plasma experiments.

**No controlled human clinical trials establish safety, benefit, dose-response, dependence liability, or overdose risk.** A 2026 case report documented severe withdrawal after intentional use, which makes conservative harm-reduction framing necessary.

## Key Points

- Mitragynine pseudoindoxyl is not traditional kratom leaf.
- It is related to 7-hydroxymitragynine through rearrangement chemistry.
- It has high mu-opioid receptor affinity in preclinical systems.
- It may form from 7-hydroxymitragynine under human plasma experimental conditions.
- Human data are limited to case reports, surveillance, and indirect pharmacology.
- It appears in some unregulated products misleadingly marketed as kratom or kratom-adjacent.

## What Mitragynine Pseudoindoxyl Is

Mitragynine pseudoindoxyl is an opioid-active kratom alkaloid derivative. It is often discussed in research because its receptor profile differs from mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine, but that research context does not make consumer products safe.

## Pharmacology

Preclinical studies describe mitragynine pseudoindoxyl as a potent mu-opioid receptor agonist with delta-opioid receptor antagonist activity. Some papers discuss G-protein-biased opioid signaling.

That mechanistic language should be interpreted carefully. Signaling bias in an assay does not prove lower overdose risk, lower withdrawal risk, or lower respiratory depression risk in humans.

## Human Evidence

There are no controlled human clinical trials of mitragynine pseudoindoxyl. The current human evidence base is extremely thin and includes metabolism work, product surveillance, and case reports.

The 2026 severe-withdrawal case report is not enough to define typical risk, but it is enough to reject casual safety claims. Dependence and withdrawal are credible clinical concerns.

## Harm Reduction

> **Do not use products containing mitragynine pseudoindoxyl.** It is significantly more potent than mitragynine and lacks basic human safety characterization.

If exposure has already happened:

- Avoid alcohol, benzodiazepines, gabapentinoids, sedating antihistamines, opioids, sleep medications, and other depressants.
- Do not drive.
- Do not use alone.
- Seek urgent medical help for slowed breathing, loss of consciousness, severe sedation, blue lips, chest pain, confusion, or collapse.
- Consider naloxone availability when opioid-acting products may be present, while recognizing that emergency care is still needed for overdose symptoms.
