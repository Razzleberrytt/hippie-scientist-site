# Dihydro-7-hydroxy Mitragynine (MGM-15): Chemistry, Pharmacology, and Emerging Risks

> **Strong disclaimer:** This page is for research literacy and harm-reduction context only. It is not medical advice, legal advice, dosing guidance, sourcing guidance, or an endorsement of use. MGM-15 is a potent opioid-acting compound with no established safe human dose and no approved medical use.

## Evidence Snapshot

MGM-15, also called dihydro-7-hydroxy mitragynine, is a semi-synthetic derivative related to [7-hydroxymitragynine](/compounds/7-hydroxymitragynine). Laboratory work and forensic alerts suggest higher opioid receptor activity than 7-OH in some preclinical systems. **No human clinical trials establish safety, therapeutic value, overdose risk, or withdrawal risk.**

The evidence grade is **Very Low** because most available information comes from chemistry, preclinical pharmacology, analytical toxicology, and market surveillance rather than controlled human studies.

## Key Points

- MGM-15 is not traditional kratom leaf.
- It is a semi-synthetic opioid-acting derivative of 7-hydroxymitragynine.
- Preclinical reports describe strong mu- and delta-opioid receptor activity.
- It has appeared in unregulated tablet products.
- No controlled human safety, pharmacokinetic, or efficacy data are available.
- Treat marketing claims about "kratom-like" or "safer opioid" effects as unsupported.

## What MGM-15 Is

MGM-15 is a chemically modified version of 7-hydroxymitragynine. It should be understood as a concentrated semi-synthetic opioid derivative, not as a whole-plant kratom preparation.

## Pharmacology

MGM-15 was studied as part of medicinal chemistry work on 7-hydroxymitragynine derivatives. Available reports indicate opioid receptor activity, especially at mu-opioid receptors and delta-opioid receptors.

That does not prove clinical usefulness. For a potent opioid-acting compound, the absence of human trials is a major safety gap. Unknowns include dose-response, duration, active metabolites, respiratory depression risk, dependence liability, withdrawal severity, drug interactions, and overdose management.

## Human Evidence

There are no controlled human clinical trials of MGM-15. There are no established therapeutic indications, no validated dosing ranges, and no clinically characterized safety margin.

**Evidence transparency matters:** the phrase "more potent than 7-OH" comes from laboratory and preclinical contexts. It should not be converted into consumer dosing assumptions.

## Harm Reduction

> **Do not use MGM-15.** It is unregulated, high potency, and poorly characterized in humans.

If exposure has already happened:

- Do not combine it with alcohol, benzodiazepines, gabapentinoids, sedating antihistamines, opioids, sleep medications, or other depressants.
- Do not drive or operate equipment.
- Do not use alone.
- Seek urgent medical care for slowed breathing, blue lips, severe sedation, confusion, collapse, chest pain, or inability to stay awake.
- Naloxone access is prudent whenever an opioid-acting product may be involved, but naloxone is not a substitute for emergency care.
