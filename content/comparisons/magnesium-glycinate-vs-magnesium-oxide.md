# Magnesium Glycinate vs Magnesium Oxide

**Cluster:** Sleep / Recovery / Anxiety  
**Search intent:** People choosing between magnesium supplement forms; often arriving from "best magnesium for sleep" or "magnesium glycinate vs oxide" queries  
**Evidence grade:** Magnesium (form-agnostic) — B | Glycinate specifically — B-C | Oxide for systemic repletion — D  
**Verdict:** Magnesium glycinate is almost always the better choice for systemic magnesium support, sleep, anxiety, and muscle recovery. Magnesium oxide has one valid use case — as a laxative — and should not be used when the goal is raising systemic magnesium levels or supporting sleep quality.

---

## The core difference: bioavailability

This comparison is less about two comparable options and more about one form that works and one that mostly doesn't — for most supplement purposes.

- **Magnesium oxide bioavailability: ~4%** (Lindberg et al. 1990, PMID: 2407766)
- **Magnesium glycinate bioavailability: ~60–80%** (chelated amino acid forms consistently outperform inorganic forms)

This is not a minor difference. If you take 500mg elemental magnesium from oxide, you absorb ~20mg. The same dose as glycinate delivers ~300–400mg absorbed. To get equivalent systemic magnesium from oxide, you would need to take 10–15x the dose — which would cause severe diarrhea.

**Why is oxide so poorly absorbed?**  
Magnesium oxide is sparingly soluble in water. It requires stomach acid to ionize into Mg²⁺ ions. Much of the magnesium passes through the gut unabsorbed, drawing water osmotically into the colon — which is exactly why it works as a laxative. This is an effect, not a bug, of the oxide form. It is not a defect of magnesium oxide for its intended use; it is a problem when that form is sold as a sleep or anxiety supplement.

**Why does glycinate absorb so much better?**  
Magnesium glycinate (magnesium chelated with two glycine molecules) is absorbed via amino acid transporter pathways in the small intestine. These are high-capacity, active transport systems that operate independently of the osmotic and solubility limitations that constrain inorganic magnesium salts. The glycine ligand also has its own calming properties (glycine is an inhibitory neurotransmitter and is used clinically for sleep quality at 3–5g doses).

## Mechanism

### Magnesium (both forms, once absorbed)
- NMDA receptor regulation: Mg²⁺ is an endogenous voltage-gated blocker of NMDA calcium channels — relevant to glutamate regulation, anxiety, and sleep
- GABA co-factor: required for GABA synthesis and receptor function
- HPA axis modulation: magnesium depletion is associated with elevated cortisol; repletion supports stress-axis normalization
- Melatonin co-factor: magnesium is required for pineal conversion of serotonin to melatonin
- 300+ enzymatic reactions (ATP synthesis, DNA repair, protein synthesis)

### Glycine bonus (glycinate form only)
- Glycine itself is an inhibitory neurotransmitter in the spinal cord and brainstem
- Clinical evidence: 3g glycine before bed reduces fatigue, improves sleep quality, reduces daytime sleepiness (Bannai et al. 2012, PMID: 22293532)
- At typical supplement doses (200–400mg elemental Mg from glycinate), the glycine content is ~800mg–1.6g — below the clinical sleep dose but potentially additive

## Evidence

### Magnesium for sleep
- Abbasi et al. 2012 (J Res Med Sci, PMID: 23853635): Magnesium 500mg/day for 8 weeks in elderly insomnia patients. Significant improvements in ISI score, sleep efficiency, sleep time, sleep onset latency, early morning awakening, serum renin, melatonin, and serum cortisol. This study used magnesium oxide — note that even with low bioavailability, some effect was observed in a deficient population.
- Zhang et al. 2022 meta-analysis: Magnesium supplementation improved sleep quality in observational studies; RCT data mixed (often limited to deficient populations).
- **Evidence rating for magnesium-for-sleep: B-C** — Evidence is strongest in magnesium-deficient populations (elderly, low dietary intake). Effect size in non-deficient healthy adults is unclear.

### Magnesium oxide as laxative
- Well-established; used clinically for constipation, bowel prep
- **Evidence rating for magnesium oxide as laxative: A-B** — Functional evidence, widely used clinically

### Magnesium glycinate specifically
- Limited trials test glycinate head-to-head vs other forms for sleep endpoints
- Generally extrapolated from magnesium category data plus the bioavailability advantage
- **Evidence rating for glycinate for sleep: B** (category evidence, high bioavailability makes it the more logical form to use; not inferior to the overall category evidence)

## Dosing

### Magnesium glycinate
- Typical dose for sleep/anxiety: 200–400mg elemental magnesium (check label — "elemental" is what matters, not the total mg per capsule)
- Glycinate capsules commonly contain 50–100mg elemental magnesium per capsule; read labels carefully
- Timing: 30–60 minutes before bed for sleep support
- Starting dose: 100–200mg elemental to assess tolerance
- Upper tolerable limit (from food + supplements): 350mg supplemental magnesium/day per NIH (above this, GI effects become likely even from well-absorbed forms)

### Magnesium oxide
- Laxative dose: 400–1200mg elemental magnesium oxide (lower end for gentle effect, higher for full bowel prep effect)
- Do not use as a daily sleep or anxiety supplement — the dose required to absorb meaningful magnesium will reliably cause diarrhea

## Safety

### Shared cautions
- **Kidney disease:** magnesium is renally cleared; patients with CKD stage 3+ should not supplement without medical guidance — hypermagnesemia risk
- **Medication interactions:** magnesium can interfere with absorption of some antibiotics (quinolones, tetracyclines), bisphosphonates, and levothyroxine — space by 2+ hours
- **Neuromuscular blockers:** IV magnesium potentiates effects; oral supplementation at typical doses is lower risk but clinician review is warranted pre-procedure

### Glycinate-specific
- Well-tolerated; GI side effects are rare at typical doses
- Glycine may theoretically interact with clozapine (glycine transporter mechanism); highly unlikely at supplement doses but noted

### Oxide-specific
- Diarrhea and abdominal cramping are dose-dependent and expected
- Not appropriate for long-term daily use unless prescribed as a laxative

## Cost

| | Magnesium Glycinate | Magnesium Oxide |
|---|---|---|
| Typical retail price | $15–30/month | $5–10/month |
| Bioavailability | ~60–80% | ~4% |
| Elemental Mg absorbed (400mg dose) | ~240–320mg | ~16mg |
| Cost per mg actually absorbed | Low | Very high |
| Sleep/anxiety utility | High | Low |
| Laxative utility | Low | High |

Magnesium oxide appears cheaper by unit cost but is dramatically less cost-effective for systemic magnesium support. If your goal is anything other than a laxative effect, magnesium oxide is poor value.

## Stack context

### Magnesium glycinate in sleep stack
- **Magnesium glycinate + L-theanine:** synergistic — both modulate NMDA/GABA, complementary onset profiles (theanine is faster, magnesium is systemic baseline support)
- **Magnesium glycinate + melatonin:** widely used combination; magnesium as co-factor for melatonin synthesis plus supplemental melatonin
- **Magnesium glycinate + ashwagandha:** stress/sleep overlap — ashwagandha for HPA axis support, magnesium for systemic mineral baseline
- **Glycine (standalone):** if you want to specifically amplify the glycine component, separate glycine powder (3–5g) has direct RCT evidence for sleep quality

### Magnesium oxide
- No useful stack applications for metabolic or sleep goals
- Can combine with simethicone (as in some OTC products) for constipation with gas
