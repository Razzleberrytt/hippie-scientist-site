# Mitragynine Pseudoindoxyl: Chemistry, Pharmacology, and Emerging Risks

> **Strong safety notice:** This article is educational harm-reduction content, not medical advice. Mitragynine pseudoindoxyl is a potent opioid-acting compound with no established safe dose and no FDA-approved use. Do not use products containing mitragynine pseudoindoxyl. Seek emergency care for slowed breathing, loss of consciousness, severe sedation, chest pain, blue lips, or inability to stay awake.

## Evidence Snapshot

- Mitragynine pseudoindoxyl is a rearrangement product related to [7-hydroxymitragynine](/articles/7-hydroxymitragynine/).
- It has shown high mu-opioid receptor activity in preclinical and in vitro studies.
- Some laboratory work describes G-protein-biased signaling, but this does not prove human safety.
- No controlled human clinical trials establish safe use, therapeutic value, pharmacokinetics, or long-term risk.
- It has been detected in unregulated products and is now tracked by forensic and public health groups.
- Overall evidence grade for human effects: **Very Low**.

> **Bottom line:** Mitragynine pseudoindoxyl should be treated as a high-risk, poorly characterized opioid derivative. It is not equivalent to traditional kratom leaf and should not be used as a supplement or self-treatment.

## What Mitragynine Pseudoindoxyl Is

Mitragynine pseudoindoxyl is an opioid-active compound formed through rearrangement chemistry from 7-hydroxymitragynine. It can be produced synthetically and has also been reported as a product of 7-hydroxymitragynine transformation in human plasma under experimental conditions.

The compound has become more relevant because forensic and market surveillance reports have described products containing mitragynine pseudoindoxyl, sometimes alongside 7-hydroxymitragynine and sometimes marketed in ways that blur the distinction between traditional kratom and concentrated opioid-active derivatives.

For parent-compound context, see [mitragynine](/articles/mitragynine/), [7-hydroxymitragynine](/articles/7-hydroxymitragynine/), and the [mitragynine vs 7-hydroxymitragynine comparison](/compare/mitragynine-vs-7-hydroxymitragynine/).

## Background and Chemistry

Mitragynine pseudoindoxyl is part of the kratom pseudoindoxyl family. It can arise from 7-hydroxymitragynine through a rearrangement to a spiropseudoindoxyl core. Chemistry papers have examined its stereochemistry and structural behavior, while pharmacology papers have explored its opioid receptor profile.

## Pharmacology and Receptor Activity

Mitragynine pseudoindoxyl has been reported to act as a potent mu-opioid receptor agonist and delta-opioid receptor antagonist in preclinical studies. Some research has emphasized G-protein-biased signaling and limited beta-arrestin recruitment.

That mechanistic language requires caution. In vitro signaling bias does not reliably translate into lower overdose risk, lower dependence risk, or lower respiratory risk in humans. For YMYL interpretation, the absence of controlled human safety data should dominate the conclusion.

## Clinical Evidence

There are no published controlled human clinical trials showing that mitragynine pseudoindoxyl is safe or effective for any medical use. Human evidence is limited to case reports, forensic monitoring, and product-market surveillance.

A 2026 case report described severe early-onset withdrawal after intentional use. Another 2026 case report described withdrawal management in the setting of reported regular mitragynine pseudoindoxyl use. These reports do not establish prevalence, but they are strong enough to support a conservative warning: dependence and withdrawal are plausible and clinically important risks.

## Safety and Risks

Mitragynine pseudoindoxyl should be treated as a potent opioid-acting compound. Risks include respiratory depression, profound sedation, physical dependence, withdrawal, impaired judgment, accidental overdose, and dangerous interactions with other depressants.

Higher-risk combinations include alcohol, benzodiazepines, gabapentinoids, sedating antihistamines, opioids, sleep medications, muscle relaxants, and other central nervous system depressants. People with sleep apnea, chronic lung disease, liver disease, opioid tolerance changes, prior substance use disorder, or concurrent sedating medications may face additional risk.

> **Repeated harm-reduction warning:** Do not use mitragynine pseudoindoxyl. If exposure already happened, do not combine it with depressants, do not drive, and do not use alone. Naloxone access is prudent when an opioid-acting compound may be involved, but emergency medical evaluation is still necessary for overdose symptoms.

## Product and Labeling Concerns

Public health and forensic reports describe emerging kratom-related products containing high concentrations of 7-hydroxymitragynine and/or mitragynine pseudoindoxyl. Product labeling may use terms such as "kratom," "7-OH," "research," or branded names without clearly communicating the pharmacological difference between traditional leaf and concentrated opioid-active derivatives.

| Product issue | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Mislabeling | Consumers may not know they are taking a potent opioid-active derivative |
| Variable content | Tablets, liquids, and powders may contain inconsistent amounts |
| Mixed derivatives | Products may include 7-OH, MP, MGM-15, or other compounds |
| Limited testing | Routine screening may not identify every derivative |
| No clinical labeling | No standardized contraindications, interactions, or overdose guidance |

## Key Takeaways

> **Key takeaways:** Mitragynine pseudoindoxyl is a potent opioid-active compound related to 7-hydroxymitragynine. Its laboratory pharmacology is not a safety guarantee. Human evidence is extremely limited, withdrawal case reports exist, and unregulated product labeling may obscure exposure risk.

## Conclusion

Mitragynine pseudoindoxyl has important pharmacological activity in opioid receptor systems, but the human evidence base is too thin to support therapeutic or safety claims. Its appearance in consumer products is a harm-reduction concern because product composition, dose consistency, dependence liability, and overdose risk remain poorly characterized. A conservative evidence-first interpretation is to avoid use and treat any exposure as medically relevant when symptoms occur.
