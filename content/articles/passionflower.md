## Introduction

Passionflower (*Passiflora incarnata*) is a climbing vine native to the southeastern United States and widely naturalized across South America and southern Europe. Its therapeutic use for anxiety, restlessness, and insomnia predates modern clinical research by centuries — indigenous North American populations used passionflower as a sedative, and European colonizers adopted the plant into folk medicine for "nervous conditions." Contemporary pharmacological research has identified a multi-compound GABAergic mechanism underlying these traditional applications, and a small but growing body of randomized controlled trials supports its anxiolytic and sleep-promoting efficacy.

What distinguishes passionflower from many other herbal anxiolytics is an active comparison RCT against an established pharmaceutical — Akhondzadeh et al. (2001) compared passionflower directly to oxazepam (a benzodiazepine) for generalized anxiety disorder and found equivalent efficacy on anxiety outcomes, with passionflower causing less impairment on job performance. This benzodiazepine-comparative design, rare in botanical research, provides a clinically meaningful reference point for evaluating passionflower's effect magnitude.

This review covers the active chemistry, GABAergic mechanisms, key clinical trials, dosage protocols, and the safety and interaction profile relevant to clinical use.

For search intent, passionflower sits in a useful but narrow lane. The evidence supports cautious interest in passionflower for anxiety, pre-procedure nervousness, and sleep quality, especially when the problem is mental arousal rather than circadian timing. It does not support treating passionflower as a substitute for psychotherapy, SSRIs/SNRIs, benzodiazepine taper planning, or sleep-disorder evaluation. Recent reviews of *P. incarnata* emphasize that the clinical literature is promising but still limited by small samples, short trial durations, and heterogeneous preparations [9,10].

## Traditional Context and Evidence Boundaries

Passionflower has a long folk-medicine history for nervous restlessness, mild insomnia, and tension states. That traditional use is relevant because modern clinical trials have mostly tested the same broad use pattern: calming, preoperative anxiety, dental anxiety, generalized anxiety symptoms, and sleep quality [1,2,6,7,11,12]. The traditional record should not be treated as proof, but it helps explain why the modern research questions cluster around GABAergic calm rather than stimulation, endurance, or immune support.

The clinical evidence is best described as "small but coherent." Passionflower has several randomized trials, including comparisons with oxazepam, lorazepam, and midazolam in anxiety-adjacent settings [1,7,12]. However, most trials are short, many are procedural rather than chronic anxiety trials, and product standardization is inconsistent. That is why a conservative article should say "may reduce anxiety symptoms in specific short-term contexts" rather than "treats anxiety."

For readers searching "passionflower for sleep," the same discipline applies. The strongest sleep-specific human data include a small tea study and a polysomnography trial in insomnia disorder [2,6]. Those studies support sleep-quality interest, but they do not prove that passionflower fixes sleep apnea, restless legs, medication-induced insomnia, circadian rhythm delay, or severe chronic insomnia.

## Taxonomy and Active Chemistry

*Passiflora incarnata* belongs to the Passifloraceae family and is the species with the most clinical evidence; it should be distinguished from *P. caerulea* and other ornamental species, which have different alkaloid profiles. The aerial parts (leaves, stems, and flower tops) are used medicinally and harvested before or during flowering.

### Flavonoids: Chrysin and Vitexin

The primary bioactive compounds in *P. incarnata* are flavonoids. **Chrysin** (5,7-dihydroxyflavone) is the most pharmacologically studied and acts as a positive allosteric modulator at GABA-A receptors, binding the benzodiazepine recognition site at the α/γ subunit interface — the same site as benzodiazepine drugs. In radioligand binding studies, chrysin displaces diazepam from GABA-A receptors with measurable affinity, though lower than pharmaceutical benzodiazepines. The practical implication is that chrysin's anxiolytic effect operates through the same receptor mechanism as diazepam but with partial agonism, producing sedation and anxiety reduction proportional to the lower binding affinity.

**Vitexin** and **isovitexin** (C-glycosylflavones) are present in higher concentrations than chrysin and are commonly used as marker compounds in quality-control discussions. The clinical relevance of each individual flavonoid remains uncertain because human trials test whole-herb preparations, not isolated vitexin, isovitexin, or chrysin [8,9].

### Benzoflavone Moiety (BZF)

A benzoflavone compound present in *P. incarnata* (distinct from the dietary flavonoid benzoflavone) has been characterized as having MAO-inhibitory properties in addition to GABA-A activity — potentially contributing to the mood-supportive aspect of passionflower's effects alongside its sedative profile.

### Alkaloids: Harmane and Harmine

*P. incarnata* contains trace amounts of β-carboline alkaloids including harmane and harmine, which are MAO inhibitors. These are present at very low concentrations in standard preparations and are unlikely to produce clinically significant MAO inhibition at normal doses, but they represent a theoretically relevant pharmacological constituent that warrants consideration in drug interaction discussions.

## Mechanism of Action

### GABA-A Receptor Positive Allosteric Modulation

The central anxiolytic mechanism of passionflower is positive allosteric modulation of GABA-A receptors, mediated primarily by chrysin and other flavonoids binding at benzodiazepine-site subunit configurations. As with valerian's valerenic acid, passionflower flavonoids enhance GABA-A receptor function by increasing chloride channel opening probability in the presence of GABA — producing neuronal hyperpolarization, reduced excitability, sedation, and anxiolysis.

The specificity for benzodiazepine-site binding over other GABA-A modulatory sites has implications for tolerance: while classical benzodiazepines produce receptor downregulation and cognitive impairment with chronic use, partial benzodiazepine-site agonists at lower efficacy tend to produce less tolerance and minimal cognitive impairment. Passionflower's use in the Akhondzadeh 2001 trial without job-performance impairment is consistent with this lower-efficacy agonist profile.

### Adenosine Pathway

Vitexin and isovitexin from *P. incarnata* have demonstrated adenosine receptor binding in in vitro studies. Adenosine A1 receptors in the brain are inhibitory — activation decreases neuronal firing, producing sedation and sleep pressure. This adenosinergic component may contribute to passionflower's sleep-promoting effect independent of the GABA-A mechanism.

### MAO Inhibition (β-Carboline Contribution)

The trace β-carboline content (harmane, harmine) provides mild, reversible MAO-A inhibitory activity that may contribute to serotonin availability and mood support. This mechanism is secondary to the GABAergic pathway in terms of passionflower's primary anxiolytic effect, but may explain why some users report improved mood alongside the anxiolytic benefit.

## Clinical Evidence

### Generalized Anxiety Disorder: Akhondzadeh et al. 2001

The landmark anxiolytic RCT for passionflower is Akhondzadeh et al. (2001, PMID 11679026), which enrolled 36 Iranian adults with DSM-IV generalized anxiety disorder in a double-blind trial comparing passionflower extract (45 drops/day) to oxazepam (30 mg/day) over 28 days. Anxiety was assessed weekly using the Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale (HAM-A).

Both treatments produced equivalent reductions in HAM-A scores by the end of the trial — passionflower was non-inferior to oxazepam for the primary anxiety outcome. However, the passionflower group showed significantly lower impairment of job performance in the first week of treatment, while oxazepam was associated with earlier-onset sedation and significant impairment of daytime functioning during the initial phase. This superiority on functional outcome — not just anxiety scale scores — is clinically meaningful and represents one of the strongest arguments for passionflower as a first-line anxiolytic for mild-to-moderate GAD in patients for whom daytime performance matters.

### Sleep Quality: Lee et al. 2020

Lee and colleagues (2020, PMID 31714321) conducted a study specifically examining the effects of *P. incarnata* extract on polysomnographic sleep parameters in adults with insomnia disorder — a methodological advance over previous studies relying solely on subjective sleep questionnaires. The study enrolled adults with formal insomnia diagnosis and administered passionflower extract versus placebo over 2 weeks.

The passionflower group showed significant improvements in total sleep time, sleep efficiency, wake after sleep onset (WASO), and REM sleep proportion. Notably, slow-wave sleep (SWS) also showed a trend toward improvement. These polysomnographic findings provide objective validation for the subjective sleep improvements reported in earlier studies, linking passionflower's GABA-A mechanism to measurable changes in sleep architecture rather than simply altered sleep perception.

### Subjective Sleep Quality: Ngan and Conduit 2011

Ngan and Conduit (2011, PMID 21294203) examined the effects of passionflower tea on sleep quality in a smaller crossover study (41 healthy adults), comparing one week of passionflower tea (dosage equivalent to ~200 mg dried extract) versus parsley herb tea as active placebo. The Sleep Quality Index (from daily diary) was significantly better in the passionflower week versus placebo, though objective actigraphy data showed only trending differences. The relatively low dose (from tea) combined with the significant subjective outcome supports passionflower as having meaningful sleep-quality effects even at modest supplemental amounts.

### Preoperative Anxiety: Movafegh et al. 2008

Movafegh and colleagues (2008, PMID 18499602) conducted a double-blind RCT of passionflower premedication versus placebo in 60 adults undergoing surgery under spinal anesthesia. Passionflower (500 mg administered orally 90 minutes before surgery) significantly reduced preoperative anxiety on the Amsterdam Preoperative Anxiety Scale compared to placebo, without producing sedation or respiratory depression at induction. This acute single-dose study demonstrates that passionflower produces meaningful anxiolysis within 90 minutes of a large single dose — confirming pharmacologically relevant GABA-A activity with acute-onset kinetics.

### Opioid Withdrawal Support: Akhondzadeh et al. 2001

In a parallel study by the same group (PMID 11815323), passionflower extract combined with clonidine outperformed clonidine alone for opioid withdrawal symptom management in 65 patients, specifically reducing psychological withdrawal symptoms (anxiety, irritability, restlessness) without affecting the physical withdrawal symptom profile addressed by clonidine. While outside the primary sleep/anxiety application, this study demonstrates that passionflower's anxiolytic mechanism operates in pharmacologically demanding contexts.

### Dental Anxiety and Procedure-Adjacent Use

Two dental-context trials help clarify passionflower's acute calming niche. In periodontal treatment, oral *Passiflora incarnata* reduced dental anxiety compared with placebo in adults undergoing care [11]. In a dental extraction study, passionflower showed anxiety control broadly comparable to midazolam while preserving a different sedation profile [12]. These studies should not be overgeneralized to chronic anxiety disorders, but they support the more specific long-tail keyword target: passionflower may be relevant for short-term, procedure-related anxiety when sedation risk and clinician oversight are considered.

The procedural literature also reinforces a safety boundary. If passionflower is being considered before surgery, dental sedation, anesthesia, or other procedures, the clinician should know about it. The question is not only whether passionflower "works"; it is whether it stacks with sedatives, anesthetics, alcohol, antihistamines, or other calming agents in a way that changes impairment risk.

## Dosage and Forms

### Effective Dose Range

- **Anxiety:** 200–500 mg of dried aerial parts extract (standardized to 3.5–4% isovitexin or total flavonoids), taken 1–3 times daily. The Akhondzadeh 2001 dosage of 45 drops (tincture) is approximately equivalent to 300–400 mg dried extract.
- **Sleep:** 300–500 mg taken 30–60 minutes before bed. Higher doses within this range may be necessary for sleep onset benefit.

### Onset

The Movafegh preoperative trial establishes meaningful acute anxiolysis within 90 minutes of a single large dose, confirming faster onset than adaptogens like Bacopa or Rhodiola. For ongoing anxiety management, effects are reported to stabilize over 1–2 weeks.

### Forms

- **Standardized extract capsules** (most consistent): standardize to total flavonoids or isovitexin content
- **Tinctures (1:8 extract in 45% ethanol)**: 1–4 mL doses, used in the Akhondzadeh trials; allows flexible dosing
- **Teas**: low but variable dose; may be effective at 1–2 g dried herb per cup as shown in the Ngan & Conduit study; most suitable for mild, ongoing use

### Product Selection Checklist

For an evidence-aligned passionflower supplement, the label should identify *Passiflora incarnata* specifically, list the plant part, and disclose whether the product is a tea, tincture, dried herb powder, or standardized extract. Species clarity matters because ornamental and edible passionflower relatives are not interchangeable with the clinical literature on *P. incarnata* [8,9].

Prefer products that:

- Identify *Passiflora incarnata* by species name.
- Use aerial parts rather than unspecified plant material.
- Disclose dose per serving and serving timing.
- Avoid disease-treatment claims.
- Avoid combining passionflower with multiple sedatives before individual tolerance is known.

For "best passionflower supplement for anxiety" searches, the answer is not simply the highest milligram dose. The more useful filter is transparent species identity, conservative dosing, and a clean safety context.

## Safety and Interactions

### General Tolerability

Passionflower has a favorable safety profile in trials up to 28 days. The most common adverse effects are drowsiness (particularly at higher doses), dizziness, and confusion in rare cases. In the Akhondzadeh 2001 trial, passionflower caused significantly less daytime sedation and impairment than oxazepam — an important tolerability advantage over benzodiazepines.

Clinical reviews generally describe passionflower as well tolerated in short-term trials, but they also emphasize the limits of the evidence base: small studies, short follow-up, and limited pregnancy and lactation data [9,10]. The most practical adverse-effect screen is sedation, dizziness, impaired coordination, paradoxical agitation, or unusual confusion.

There are case reports of adverse events in individuals using passionflower-containing products, though causality and product composition are not always clear. Because supplements can vary in species identity, extraction method, and co-ingredients, unexplained rash, jaundice, severe dizziness, confusion, or allergic symptoms should prompt discontinuation and medical evaluation.

### Drug Interactions

Passionflower's interaction profile is dominated by additive CNS depression:

- **Benzodiazepines and non-benzodiazepine hypnotics** — binding at the same GABA-A benzodiazepine site creates additive sedation; the combination is contraindicated without physician oversight
- **Barbiturates, opioids, alcohol** — CNS depression risk; avoid combination
- **Anticoagulants (warfarin)** — passionflower may have mild anticoagulant activity; INR monitoring warranted
- **MAO inhibitors** — β-carboline content of passionflower produces weak MAO inhibition; combining with pharmaceutical MAOIs (phenelzine, tranylcypromine) or with other β-carboline-containing plants raises theoretical interaction risk
- **Antiplatelet drugs** — some animal evidence for platelet aggregation inhibition; monitor if combining with aspirin or clopidogrel

### Who Should Be More Cautious

People who take benzodiazepines, Z-drugs, opioids, sedating antihistamines, antipsychotics, barbiturates, alcohol, or multiple calming supplements should treat passionflower as an interaction question, not a casual tea. Use before dental procedures, surgery, or anesthesia should be disclosed because procedural studies involve supervised settings, not unsupervised stacking [7,11,12].

Pregnancy and breastfeeding remain conservative avoid categories unless a qualified clinician recommends otherwise. The issue is not a proven teratogenic signal in humans; it is the absence of reassuring human safety data, combined with traditional and theoretical concerns around uterine activity at high exposures [9].

## How to Use

**For anxiety management:** 200–400 mg standardized extract capsule in the morning, repeated if needed in the afternoon for persistent anxiety. Unlike benzodiazepines, passionflower does not require escalating doses and does not produce the daytime impairment characteristic of benzodiazepine daytime use.

**For sleep:** 300–500 mg extract 30–60 minutes before bed. Particularly useful for individuals whose sleep difficulty is driven by racing thoughts or psychological arousal rather than circadian rhythm disruption.

**Combination strategies:** Passionflower combines synergistically with valerian (complementary GABAergic mechanisms, different receptor binding sites) and lemon balm (adenosinergic and GABAergic activity). European combination products have demonstrated better efficacy than individual herbs in some trials.

---

## Contraindications

**Absolute:** Concurrent benzodiazepine therapy without physician supervision; first trimester of pregnancy (uterotonic alkaloid activity at high doses, historically used as an abortifacient in traditional medicine).

**Relative:** Second/third trimester pregnancy; breastfeeding (limited data); known coagulation disorder; pre-operative use within 48 hours of general anesthesia; driving or operating machinery when using sedative doses.

---

## Outlook

Passionflower's clinical evidence profile — an active comparison RCT showing non-inferiority to a benzodiazepine, a polysomnographic study confirming objective sleep architecture improvement, and a mechanistic characterization linking chrysin to GABA-A benzodiazepine-site binding — places it among the best-characterized herbal anxiolytics. Its functional tolerability advantage over benzodiazepines (preserved daytime performance) is particularly relevant for working adults or students for whom daytime function is a priority alongside anxiety management.

The remaining evidence gaps include larger and longer RCTs in diverse populations, dose-response characterization, and comparative trials against modern first-line anxiolytics (SSRIs, SNRIs) for sustained GAD management. Within its validated scope — mild-to-moderate anxiety and sleep-onset difficulty — passionflower represents an evidence-supported choice with a genuinely favorable risk-benefit profile.
