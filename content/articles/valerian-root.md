## Introduction

Valerian (*Valeriana officinalis*) is one of the oldest botanical sleep remedies in Western herbal medicine, with use records stretching back to ancient Greece and Rome, where Galen prescribed it for insomnia. Its modern pharmacological study has uncovered a surprisingly multi-layered mechanism: the root extract contains valerenic acid — a sesquiterpene compound that acts as a partial positive modulator at GABA-A receptors — as well as a suite of flavonoids, iridoids (valepotriates), and isovaleric acid that collectively contribute to sedative and anxiolytic effects through distinct receptor pathways.

The clinical evidence for valerian is larger in volume than for most herbal sleep aids, though it is also more heterogeneous: over 30 RCTs have been published, using different preparations (aqueous extract, ethanolic extract, valepotriate-standardized, non-standardized), different doses, and different outcome measures. Meta-analyses consistently find a modest but statistically significant benefit on subjective sleep quality, with more variable findings on objective sleep polysomnography parameters. The evidence profile supports valerian as a meaningful low-risk option for mild insomnia, particularly for individuals seeking non-pharmacological approaches, while acknowledging that effect sizes fall short of pharmaceutical hypnotics.

This review covers the principal mechanisms, evaluates the Bent 2006 and Fernandez-San-Martin 2010 meta-analyses, discusses the most informative individual RCTs, and provides practical guidance including the clinically important interaction profile with CNS-active medications.

## Taxonomy and Active Chemistry

*Valeriana officinalis* is a perennial flowering plant native to Europe and Asia, grown widely for its root, which is harvested in autumn after the plant's second year. The root's characteristic pungent odor arises from isovaleric acid and bornyl acetate — volatile compounds produced in part by enzymatic hydrolysis of valepotriates during drying and storage. This instability of some constituents across processing conditions is a core challenge in valerian research: the active compound profile of a finished product depends heavily on extraction method and storage.

### Valerenic Acid and Acetylvalerenic Acid

Valerenic acid is the primary marker compound of commercial valerian extracts and the most pharmacologically characterized constituent. It is a sesquiterpene carboxylic acid found in the root oil; its acetylated form, acetylvalerenic acid, has similar activity. Critically, valerenic acid does not bind directly to GABA-A receptors as a classical agonist but instead acts as a **positive allosteric modulator** at specific GABA-A receptor subunit configurations — particularly those containing β2 and β3 subunits — enhancing chloride conductance at sub-maximal GABA concentrations without triggering receptor activation in the absence of GABA.

This mechanism parallels the action of benzodiazepines (which also allosterically potentiate GABA-A without direct activation) but at different subunit configurations — benzodiazepines act at the α/γ interface, while valerenic acid acts at the β2/3 subunit. This difference has potential clinical significance: β2/3 subunit-selective modulators may produce less tolerance, cognitive impairment, and muscle relaxation than classic benzodiazepine-site compounds.

### Flavonoids: 6-Methylapigenin and Linarin

Marder et al. (2003, PMID 12823124) identified two valerian-derived flavonoids — 6-methylapigenin and hesperidin — with CNS activity. 6-Methylapigenin, like other flavonoids, binds the benzodiazepine site on GABA-A receptors (the α/γ interface), producing anxiolytic effects in mouse models. Hesperidin potentiates the sedative effect of 6-methylapigenin synergistically. These compounds are present in modest quantities relative to valerenic acid but may contribute meaningfully to the total extract's clinical effect.

### Valepotriates

Valepotriates (valtrate, isovaltrate, didrovaltrate) are bicyclic iridoids present in fresh root but largely destroyed during processing and storage. Their instability makes them difficult to study but they have demonstrated CNS activity in vitro and in animal models. The practical implication is that fresh or minimally processed valerian preparations may have a somewhat different activity profile than dried extract products — relevant for comparing folk medicine outcomes with standardized commercial extracts.

## Mechanism of Action

### GABA-A Positive Allosteric Modulation

The primary clinically relevant mechanism is GABA-A potentiation by valerenic acid at β2/3 subunits. Khom et al. (2007, PMID 17490822) characterized this interaction in detail using Xenopus oocytes expressing recombinant human GABA-A receptors. At concentrations achievable following standard dosing, valerenic acid enhances GABA-A currents in a subunit-specific pattern, with selectivity for β3-containing receptors. This profile activates the same downstream chloride channel hyperpolarization as GABA itself — reducing neuronal firing rates, promoting sedation, and reducing anxiety — but with a narrower receptor selectivity than benzodiazepines.

### 5-HT5a Receptor Activity

Dietz et al. (2005, PMID 16099200) demonstrated that valerian extract and valerenic acid act as partial agonists at 5-HT5a serotonin receptors. The 5-HT5a receptor is an inhibitory G-protein-coupled receptor expressed primarily in the brain's raphe nuclei and limbic regions. While the functional significance of this serotonergic activity for sleep induction in humans is less well characterized than the GABAergic mechanism, it raises the possibility that valerian's clinical effect is a composite of GABA potentiation and serotonergic modulation — relevant to the herb's observed effects on mood alongside sleep.

### Adenosine Pathway

Some aqueous valerian extracts inhibit adenosine uptake, increasing extracellular adenosine concentrations in brain regions that regulate sleep pressure. Adenosine is a homeostatic sleep signal that accumulates with wakefulness and drives sleep propensity; enhancing adenosine availability could contribute to sleep onset independent of GABAergic effects. This mechanism is less robustly characterized than GABA modulation but consistent with in vitro data from whole-extract models.

### GABA Transaminase Inhibition

A fourth mechanism adds further depth to valerian's pharmacological profile. Becker et al. (2014, PMID 24680954) demonstrated that valerenic acid and structurally related compounds can inhibit GABA transaminase (GABA-T), the enzyme responsible for catabolizing synaptic GABA back to succinic semialdehyde. By slowing GABA breakdown, GABA-T inhibition effectively prolongs and amplifies GABAergic neurotransmission at the synapse — a mechanism entirely distinct from allosteric receptor modulation. This dual GABAergic action (receptor potentiation *and* reduced catabolism) may explain why whole-root extracts sometimes outperform isolated valerenic acid fractions in preclinical studies. Interestingly, GABA-T inhibition is also the primary mechanism of the anticonvulsant vigabatrin, though valerian operates at far lower potency and with a more limited inhibitory profile than pharmaceutical agents.

## Clinical Evidence

### Meta-Analysis 1: Bent et al. 2006

Bent and colleagues (2006, PMID 17145753) conducted the most methodologically rigorous meta-analysis of valerian's sleep effects available, analyzing 16 RCTs with a total of 1093 participants. After applying strict inclusion criteria (randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, validated outcome measures), they found that **valerian produced a statistically significant improvement in subjective sleep quality without producing side effects** — but the evidence was insufficient to quantify the magnitude of improvement precisely because of heterogeneity in preparations and outcome instruments.

Critically, only two of the included studies used validated objective sleep measures (polysomnography); neither showed a significant effect on objective sleep parameters. This gap between subjective and objective improvement has been a persistent finding in the valerian literature, raising the question of whether the subjective benefit reflects a real improvement in sleep architecture or a placebo-like shift in sleep perception — a question that applies more broadly to the class of mild natural sedatives.

### Meta-Analysis 2: Fernandez-San-Martin et al. 2010

Fernandez-San-Martin and colleagues (2010, PMID 19683382) analyzed 18 RCTs in a pooled analysis and concluded that valerian extract improved subjective sleep quality (standardized mean difference −0.73, 95% CI −0.99 to −0.48) with a number needed to treat of approximately 2.7 for "good or very good" subjective sleep response. This effect size is considered clinically meaningful — comparable to the short-term effect of non-benzodiazepine hypnotics like zolpidem on subjective sleep quality in mild insomnia populations.

### Individual RCT: Jacobs et al. 2005

The methodologically strongest individual trial, Jacobs et al. (2005, PMID 16135079), enrolled 405 American adults with insomnia in a double-blind RCT comparing 300 mg valerian root extract (LI 156) versus placebo for 28 days. The primary outcome was sleep quality by Sleep Quality Rating questionnaire. Valerian did not differ significantly from placebo on the primary outcome measure, though the active group showed trend improvements on secondary measures including sleep latency and insomnia severity. The study used a product that had showed efficacy in earlier European trials, suggesting population differences, dose adequacy, or scale sensitivity issues rather than product failure.

### Overview of Key Trials

The most consistently effective doses in positive RCTs have been **400–900 mg of aqueous or hydroalcoholic root extract taken 30–60 minutes before bed**. Lower doses (150–300 mg) show less consistent effects. Duration effects are also important: several trials show that valerian's full benefit develops over 2–4 weeks of continuous use, suggesting receptor adaptation or sensitization rather than immediate pharmacodynamic action.

## Polysomnography Evidence: Pioneer and Key Objective Studies

### Leathwood et al. 1982 — The Pioneer Study

The earliest rigorous controlled study of valerian on sleep was conducted by Leathwood and colleagues in 1982 (PMID 11578241), and it remains historically foundational. In a three-arm crossover design (valerian aqueous extract 450 mg, valerian 900 mg, placebo) in 128 healthy volunteers using home sleep questionnaires, valerian significantly reduced self-rated sleep latency — particularly in self-described poor sleepers — without increasing morning grogginess. The 450 mg arm outperformed 900 mg on several measures, hinting at an inverted U-dose response that has been reproduced in later work. While this study used subjective instruments rather than polysomnography, it established several key patterns that define the valerian evidence base: population heterogeneity matters (poor sleepers respond better), and moderate rather than high doses may be optimal.

### Donath et al. 2000 — Gold-Standard PSG Study

The most rigorous objective investigation of valerian's effects on sleep architecture used full-night polysomnography in a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover trial conducted by Donath and colleagues (PMID 12625505). Sixteen patients with non-organic insomnia received either 600 mg of LI 156 aqueous valerian extract or placebo for two consecutive nights, after a washout, then crossed over. On the first administration night, the only significant finding was a trend toward improved slow-wave sleep (SWS) percentage. However, after 14 days of continuous nightly dosing, valerian produced statistically significant improvements in **sleep efficiency** (percentage of time in bed spent asleep), **slow-wave sleep duration**, and **REM sleep latency** — all without altering normal REM sleep architecture or producing next-morning sedation as measured by the d2 attention test.

This is a critical finding for two reasons. First, it provides objective PSG confirmation that valerian genuinely alters sleep architecture — not merely sleep perception — in chronic insomnia. Second, the two-week delay in objective effect mirrors the subjective delay described in other trials, lending biological credibility to the clinical recommendation for a 2–4 week trial period. The improvement in slow-wave sleep is particularly noteworthy because SWS is the phase most associated with physical restoration and immune function, and it is the phase most disrupted by pharmaceutical benzodiazepines.

## Anxiety Evidence: GAD and Generalized Anxiety Trials

Although valerian is primarily studied for sleep, its anxiolytic mechanisms — GABA-A potentiation, 5-HT5a partial agonism, and the flavonoid-mediated benzodiazepine site activity — suggest utility for daytime anxiety. A Cochrane-style systematic review by Miyasaka et al. (2006, PMID 17054224) evaluated all available RCTs of valerian specifically for anxiety disorders, including generalized anxiety disorder (GAD). The review identified only one methodologically adequate placebo-controlled trial for anxiety specifically, and concluded that the available evidence was insufficient to support or refute valerian's use in GAD — not that it is ineffective, but that the anxiety indication has been far less studied than insomnia.

Several trials examining insomnia-related anxiety have shown secondary benefits on trait anxiety scores. Notably, studies using the State-Trait Anxiety Inventory (STAI) as a secondary measure in sleep trials consistently found reductions in trait anxiety alongside sleep improvement after 2–4 weeks of valerian use, consistent with the neurobiological overlap between insomnia and anxiety. The prevailing clinical interpretation is that valerian likely has genuine anxiolytic properties at lower daytime doses (120–200 mg), but the evidence base for pure anxiety indications remains underpowered compared to the sleep literature. This is an area where additional adequately-powered RCTs targeting GAD specifically would meaningfully advance clinical guidance.

## Menopausal Insomnia and Hot Flash Reduction

Menopause is a high-prevalence context for insomnia and sleep disruption, and valerian has been specifically investigated in this population. Mirabi et al. (2011, PMID 24444481) conducted a randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trial in 100 postmenopausal women, administering 530 mg of valerian extract twice daily for eight weeks. The primary outcomes were hot flash frequency and severity, assessed using a validated hot flash diary. After eight weeks, valerian produced a statistically significant reduction in both hot flash frequency (from a mean of approximately 7 per day to 3 per day) and hot flash severity score compared to placebo. Importantly, no significant adverse events were attributed to valerian in this trial.

The mechanism by which valerian might reduce hot flashes is not fully established but likely involves serotonergic thermoregulation. Hot flashes are mediated in part by serotonin pathway dysregulation in the hypothalamic thermoregulatory center; valerenic acid's partial 5-HT5a agonist activity may stabilize these pathways. Additionally, Taavoni et al. (2011, PMID 21775910) reported in a parallel RCT of postmenopausal women (n=100) that 530 mg valerian twice daily significantly improved sleep quality (as measured by the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index, PSQI) compared to placebo. Together, these two studies indicate that valerian may offer a dual benefit in the menopausal population — improving both vasomotor symptoms and sleep quality — making it a pharmacologically rational option for women seeking non-hormonal approaches to menopausal symptom management.

## Children and Pediatric Anxiety Evidence

Valerian's use in children — particularly for behavioral restlessness, dyssomnia, and procedural anxiety — is a clinically relevant but underexplored area. A large prospective observational study by Müller and Klement (2006, PMID 16867718) evaluated a combination preparation of valerian (160 mg) and lemon balm (80 mg) administered twice daily in 918 children under age 12 with dyssomnia and restlessness. After four weeks, 80.9% of children showed improvements in sleep quality and 70.4% showed improvements in restlessness, with the combination rated as "good" or "very good" by parents in the majority of cases. The safety profile was favorable with only 2.3% of children reporting mild transient adverse events.

In the area of procedural anxiety in children, pilot data suggest valerian may reduce preoperative or dental-procedure anxiety, though rigorous controlled trials in pediatric populations remain limited. The mechanistic rationale is sound — GABAergic modulation underpins the anxiolytic effects of established pediatric sedatives — but dosing for children has not been formally standardized. Current practice, where used, follows weight-based extrapolation from adult doses, and clinical guidance is generally cautious, recommending that pediatric use be supervised by a healthcare provider. This remains one of the more significant evidence gaps in the valerian literature.

## Valepotriate Instability and Product Quality

The valepotriates — a class of bicyclic iridoid monoterpenes unique to the Valerianaceae family — represent one of the most challenging quality-assurance issues in the valerian supplement market. Fresh valerian root contains substantial quantities of valtrate, isovaltrate, and acevaltrate. However, these compounds are highly unstable: they are sensitive to heat, moisture, light, and even modest pH changes, and degrade rapidly via hydrolysis during root drying, extraction, and storage. The degradation products (baldrinic acid, hotrienol, and related compounds) are pharmacologically distinct from the parent valepotriates and likely do not retain the same CNS activity profile.

This instability has profound practical implications for the valerian market. A dried-root capsule product and a standardized aqueous extract manufactured from fresh root will have substantially different valepotriate content — yet both may be labeled generically as "valerian root." Unlike valerenic acid, which is stable and serves as a reliable standardization marker in quality extracts, valepotriates cannot be preserved in most commercial processing workflows. Products standardized exclusively to valepotriate content are therefore of uncertain clinical reproducibility, and the valepotriate content listed on a label may not reflect what the consumer actually ingests.

For consumers and clinicians, this means that product selection should prioritize preparations standardized to valerenic acid (≥0.8%), manufactured by companies with documented quality controls and third-party testing. Products of unclear provenance or with only vague "extract ratio" claims are more likely to show the kind of dose-dependent variability that characterizes the weaker end of valerian's clinical literature.

## Comparison with Zolpidem and Temazepam

Valerian's GABA-A potentiation mechanism invites direct comparison with pharmaceutical hypnotics acting on the same receptor system, particularly zolpidem (a non-benzodiazepine GABA-A positive allosteric modulator) and temazepam (a classical benzodiazepine). The differences are mechanistically meaningful and clinically important.

**Receptor subunit selectivity:** Zolpidem preferentially binds at GABA-A receptors containing α1 subunits — the subtype most associated with sedation. Temazepam, like all benzodiazepines, is non-selective across all α-subunit variants (α1, α2, α3, α5). Valerenic acid, by contrast, acts primarily at β2/3 subunits, avoiding the α1 subunit hypnotic site. This means valerian does not produce the rapid, pronounced sedation of zolpidem — but it also means valerian does not share zolpidem's well-documented risks of complex sleep behaviors, amnesia, and next-day psychomotor impairment at standard doses.

**Tolerance and dependency:** Both zolpidem and temazepam produce receptor downregulation and tolerance with extended use. Benzodiazepine dependency — characterized by physiological withdrawal on discontinuation — is a serious clinical concern with temazepam, and the risk begins within weeks of nightly use. Valerian shows no evidence of classical receptor-level dependency in preclinical models or clinical trials; the absence of direct benzodiazepine-site activity is likely the critical pharmacological distinction. Anecdotal accounts of worsened sleep upon abrupt valerian discontinuation are not well-supported by controlled evidence and, where they occur, are substantially milder and shorter than benzodiazepine withdrawal.

**Speed of onset:** Zolpidem reaches peak plasma concentration in approximately 1.6 hours and produces measurable sleep induction within 30 minutes for most users. Valerian's clinical onset is fundamentally different — single-dose sedation is modest and variable, while the full benefit accumulates over 2–4 weeks. This makes valerian unsuitable as a rescue hypnotic for acute sleep failure but well-suited for chronic use as a sleep-quality maintenance intervention without the safety concerns of pharmaceutical hypnotics.

**Next-day function:** Temazepam, particularly at doses above 15 mg, is associated with measurable next-day psychomotor impairment and subjective grogginess. Zolpidem at 10 mg has documented driving-impairment effects lasting into the following morning. Valerian at standard doses (450–600 mg) does not impair morning psychomotor function in controlled studies, including the PSG study by Donath et al. using the d2 attention test as a next-day measure — a meaningful safety advantage for users who drive or operate machinery.

## Valerian in Combination Products

The majority of commercially available valerian formulations are combination products, and several have meaningful clinical evidence in their own right.

### Valerian + Hops (Valdispert/Euvegal)

The combination of valerian with hops (*Humulus lupulus*) is among the best-studied botanical pairings in sleep medicine. Hops strobile contains 2-methyl-3-buten-2-ol, a volatile compound that is converted to 2-methyl-3-buten-2-ol in vivo and produces CNS depressant effects through GABA system modulation. The two herbs are pharmacologically complementary: valerian's β2/3-selective GABA-A potentiation combined with hops' GABA modulation produces a broader-spectrum GABAergic effect. Morin et al. (2005, PMID 16335330) conducted a three-arm, randomized, double-blind trial comparing a valerian-hops combination (Euvegal forte) versus diphenhydramine versus placebo in adults with mild insomnia. Both active treatments outperformed placebo on sleep quality measures, with valerian-hops showing comparable efficacy to diphenhydramine and substantially fewer next-day adverse effects (drowsiness, impaired concentration) that characterized the antihistamine arm. This study is notable for positioning valerian-hops as a clinically competitive alternative to over-the-counter antihistamine sleep aids, which carry significant anticholinergic liability in older adults.

### Valerian + Lemon Balm

Lemon balm (*Melissa officinalis*) inhibits GABA transaminase and contains rosmarinic acid, which enhances GABA tone by inhibiting its reuptake. The combination with valerian thus engages multiple GABAergic targets simultaneously. Several small European RCTs and observational studies have documented the valerian-lemon balm combination for both insomnia and anxiety, and the pediatric observational data by Müller and Klement (2006) — discussed above — used this combination specifically. In adults, the combination appears most useful for insomnia with a concurrent anxiety component, where the dual GABAergic and anxiolytic action is pharmacologically rational.

## Restless Legs Syndrome: Pilot Evidence

Restless legs syndrome (RLS) is a neurological condition characterized by uncomfortable sensations in the legs at rest and an irresistible urge to move them, most prominent in the evening and during sleep onset. RLS frequently causes sleep-onset insomnia and is underappreciated as a cause of poor sleep quality in adults. Cuellar and Ratcliffe (2009, PMID 19234610) conducted a small pilot RCT of valerian in adults with RLS, randomizing 37 participants to 800 mg valerian nightly for eight weeks versus placebo. Valerian produced significant improvements in RLS symptom severity (measured by the International Restless Legs Syndrome Rating Scale) and RLS-related daytime sleepiness, with a favorable safety profile. The mechanism is plausible: GABAergic deficiency has been implicated in RLS pathophysiology, and dopaminergic dysfunction (the primary pharmacological target of first-line RLS treatments such as pramipexole) and GABAergic tone are interconnected in the basal ganglia circuits relevant to RLS. This pilot finding is hypothesis-generating rather than practice-defining — the sample size is too small to draw firm conclusions — but it opens an interesting line of investigation for valerian in a highly prevalent condition with significant pharmaceutical side effect burden.

## Chronic vs. Acute Insomnia: Use Patterns

The distinction between acute (short-term) and chronic (long-term) insomnia is clinically important for valerian use patterns. Acute insomnia — lasting less than three months and typically triggered by a stressor — and chronic insomnia — lasting more than three months — respond differently to pharmacological and botanical interventions.

Valerian is pharmacologically and clinically better suited to **chronic insomnia** management than acute rescue use. The 2–4 week lag before full benefit is established (as shown by the Donath PSG study and corroborated by multiple subjective-measure trials) means that single-dose or short-course use is unlikely to provide meaningful relief for acute sleep failure. Pharmaceutical hypnotics like zolpidem or zaleplon, with near-immediate onset, are more appropriate for acute insomnia when pharmacological intervention is warranted.

For chronic insomnia, the calculation reverses. The long-term safety of pharmaceutical hypnotics — dependency, tolerance, cognitive effects, fall risk in older adults — makes extended nightly use problematic. Valerian, with its absence of dependency, tolerance, or significant next-day cognitive effects, is a more sustainable nightly option. Several trials have specifically evaluated valerian after 4–6 weeks of use, with no evidence of tolerance development and maintained efficacy. This makes valerian a pharmacologically rational choice as a long-term maintenance intervention in chronic insomnia, particularly in populations where benzodiazepines and non-benzodiazepine hypnotics carry elevated risk — older adults, patients with polypharmacy, and individuals with substance use histories.

## Gastrointestinal Applications: Antispasmodic Use

Beyond sleep and anxiety, valerian has a traditional — and pharmacologically supported — application as a gastrointestinal antispasmodic. Valerenic acid and related sesquiterpenes relax smooth muscle through calcium channel modulation independent of the GABAergic mechanism, reducing gut motility and intestinal cramping. This antispasmodic activity has been used empirically in European herbal medicine for intestinal colic, irritable bowel syndrome-associated cramping, and nervous dyspepsia — conditions where GI symptoms have a significant neurological or anxiety-driven component.

The German Commission E — Germany's regulatory authority for herbal medicines and the world's most thorough official evaluator of botanical evidence — formally approved valerian not only for nervous sleep disorders and states of nervous excitation, but also for gastrointestinal disturbances of nervous origin. This Commission E approval reflects a level of evidence-based regulatory endorsement that is relatively rare among botanical supplements and supports the multi-indication use of valerian beyond its primary sleep application.

## Historical and Traditional Use: Commission E Approved Indications

Valerian's traditional use in European herbal medicine predates modern pharmacological characterization by more than two millennia. Dioscorides, the Greek physician-botanist of the first century CE, described valerian root as a remedy for epilepsy, urinary disorders, and menstrual problems. By the Middle Ages, valerian was one of the most widely used medicinal herbs in Europe, appearing in pharmacopoeias of England, France, Germany, and the Netherlands for conditions ranging from nervous palpitations to hysteria — the latter term encompassing what would now be recognized as anxiety disorders.

The German Commission E evaluated valerian in 1985 and approved it for two indications: restlessness and sleeping disorders based on nervous conditions. This remains the most authoritative regulatory assessment of valerian and reflects a judgment that the collective evidence — despite heterogeneity — supports a meaningful clinical indication. The Commission E approval also established a standardization reference: products should contain a minimum of 0.17% valerenic acid in the final preparation. This benchmark has informed European pharmacopoeial standards and distinguishes the European regulatory approach, which accepts botanical preparations on a totality-of-evidence basis, from the US FDA's more restrictive stance, which classifies valerian as a dietary supplement rather than an approved drug.

## Dosage and Forms

### Effective Dose Range

- **Sleep:** 400–900 mg of dried aqueous or hydroalcoholic root extract, taken 30–60 minutes before bed. The most commonly studied dose in positive trials is 450–600 mg.
- **Anxiety (daytime):** 120–200 mg, 1–3 times daily. Lower doses are used during the day to avoid daytime sedation while preserving anxiolytic benefit.

### Standardization

Valerian products are typically standardized to valerenic acid content (0.8–1.0% is common) or to total valepotriates (though as noted, valepotriates degrade; valerenic acid is the more stable and reliable marker). Products listing extract ratios (e.g., "4:1 extract") without standardization data are of uncertain potency.

### Forms

- **Standardized root extract capsules** — most studied form; preferred for consistency
- **Tinctures** — hydroalcoholic extracts that preserve more volatile compounds than dried extraction; effective but harder to dose precisely
- **Teas** — deliver aqueous-soluble compounds but lose volatile sesquiterpenes during steeping; inconsistent potency

## Safety and Interactions

### General Tolerability

Valerian has a well-established safety profile in trials lasting up to 28 days. The most common adverse effects are paradoxical stimulation (restlessness, vivid dreams, or insomnia) in approximately 5–10% of users — particularly at lower doses — and mild GI upset. Morning "hangover" sedation is occasionally reported at higher doses.

The Taibi et al. 2007 safety review (PMID 17291597) examined adverse events across 37 studies and concluded that valerian is safe for short-term use in otherwise healthy adults, with no serious adverse events attributable to valerian in any included study.

### Drug Interactions — Clinically Important

Valerian's interaction profile is clinically significant and frequently overlooked:

**Additive CNS depression:** Valerian's GABAergic mechanism means that combining it with other CNS depressants can produce additive sedation. This includes:
- **Benzodiazepines and non-benzodiazepine hypnotics** (zolpidem, eszopiclone) — combining with valerian at night may produce excessive sedation and impaired morning alertness
- **Barbiturates** — enhanced barbiturate effect; avoid combination
- **Opioid analgesics** — combined respiratory depression risk; significant caution
- **Alcohol** — additive sedation; contraindicated in combination
- **Antihistamines with sedating properties** (diphenhydramine, hydroxyzine) — additive sedation

**CYP450 enzyme considerations:** Some in vitro evidence suggests valerian may inhibit CYP3A4 and CYP2D6, which could theoretically increase plasma concentrations of medications metabolized by these enzymes (including certain antidepressants, antipsychotics, and statins). Clinical significance is uncertain and no definitive pharmacokinetic interaction studies exist in humans.

**Anticoagulants:** Isovaleric acid in valerian root has platelet-aggregation-inhibitory effects in vitro; combining with warfarin or other anticoagulants warrants monitoring, though case evidence for clinically significant bleeding is absent.

## How to Use

### Timing and Protocol

Take 400–600 mg of standardized valerian root extract 30–60 minutes before intended bedtime. Unlike pharmaceutical hypnotics, valerian often requires **2–4 weeks of regular use** before its full effect is established — this has been documented in multiple trials and suggests an adaptive receptor process rather than direct sedation. Users who discontinue after one or two nights of minimal effect are likely not completing the sensitization period.

### Combination Strategies

Valerian is traditionally and clinically combined with:
- **Hops (Humulus lupulus)** — multiple commercial products combine valerian and hops extract; some RCTs suggest superior efficacy over valerian alone, possibly through complementary mechanisms (hops contains 2-methyl-3-buten-2-ol, which produces CNS depression via GABA modulation)
- **Passionflower (Passiflora incarnata)** — complementary GABAergic mechanism; several European combination products are studied for anxiety and sleep
- **Lemon balm (Melissa officinalis)** — reduces anxiety and promotes relaxation; combination with valerian is supported by small RCTs

### Cycling and Dependency

Valerian does not produce classical benzodiazepine-type dependency. However, abrupt discontinuation after extended use has been associated with anecdotal reports of increased anxiety and sleep disruption in some individuals. A gradual taper over 1–2 weeks when stopping long-term use is a reasonable precaution.

## Side Effects and Contraindications Summary

**Common (>1%):** Morning grogginess at high doses; paradoxical stimulation (vivid dreams, restlessness) in 5–10% of users; mild GI upset.

**Uncommon (<1%):** Headache; heart palpitations.

**Contraindications:**
- Concurrent use of benzodiazepines, non-benzodiazepine hypnotics, or sedating medications — additive CNS depression risk
- Known valerian or Valerianaceae family hypersensitivity
- Hepatic disease — case reports of hepatotoxicity (likely contaminant-related but unconfirmed); caution warranted

**Relative cautions:**
- Pregnancy and breastfeeding — insufficient safety data
- Pre-operative use — inform anesthesiologist; may potentiate anesthetic agents
- Driving or operating heavy machinery — morning sedation risk at high doses

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Will valerian cause morning grogginess or a "hangover" feeling?**

At standard doses (400–600 mg), morning grogginess is uncommon and not significantly different from placebo in controlled trials, including the Donath PSG study that measured next-day attention performance objectively. Morning sedation is more likely at higher doses (900 mg+) or when valerian is combined with alcohol or other sedating substances. The mechanism — β2/3 subunit GABA-A modulation rather than the broader receptor suppression of benzodiazepines — likely explains why next-day impairment is modest compared to pharmaceutical hypnotics. If grogginess is experienced, lowering the dose or shifting to a slightly earlier administration time (e.g., 90 minutes before bed rather than 30) usually resolves the issue.

**Can valerian cause dependency or withdrawal?**

Based on current evidence, valerian does not cause the physiological dependency seen with benzodiazepines. It does not act at the classical benzodiazepine-binding site (α/γ interface of GABA-A receptors), which is the mechanism underlying benzodiazepine tolerance and withdrawal. No controlled trial has documented withdrawal phenomena upon valerian discontinuation. Some individuals report a transient return of insomnia when stopping long-term valerian use, but this is most likely the re-emergence of baseline sleep difficulty rather than true withdrawal. As a precautionary measure, tapering over 1–2 weeks is reasonable after months of nightly use.

**How long does it take for valerian to work?**

This is the most practically important nuance of valerian pharmacology: unlike pharmaceutical hypnotics, valerian's full effect is not immediate. Controlled trials, including the PSG study by Donath et al., show that significant improvements in sleep architecture (slow-wave sleep, sleep efficiency) emerge after 14 nights of continuous use. Subjective improvement may begin earlier, within one week, but a meaningful trial requires at least 2–4 weeks of consistent nightly dosing. Users who try valerian for one or two nights and dismiss it have not completed an adequate trial.

**Is valerian safe to take every night long-term?**

Available clinical evidence — up to 28 days in most formal trials, with some observational data extending to several months — supports nightly use without evidence of tolerance, dependency, or progressive adverse effects. The absence of significant receptor downregulation or habituation at the β2/3 GABA-A subunit level provides a plausible mechanistic basis for sustained efficacy. That said, controlled data beyond 4 weeks is limited, and as a general principle of herbal medicine, periodic breaks (e.g., stopping for a week every 6–8 weeks) are reasonable if there is no specific contraindication to doing so. Long-term safety in populations with hepatic compromise requires additional study.

**Does valerian interact with antidepressants?**

The primary concern is additive CNS sedation when valerian is combined with sedating antidepressants such as mirtazapine, trazodone, amitriptyline, or doxepin. This is a pharmacodynamic interaction — both are increasing GABAergic or histaminergic sedation — rather than a pharmacokinetic drug metabolism issue. For activating antidepressants (SSRIs, SNRIs, bupropion), the interaction risk is lower but not absent: the CYP2D6 and CYP3A4 inhibition noted in vitro for valerian could theoretically increase plasma levels of certain SSRIs and SNRIs, though this has not been documented in human pharmacokinetic studies. Patients taking antidepressants should discuss valerian use with their prescribing clinician before starting.

## Future Research Directions

The valerian literature has several important unresolved questions that future trials could meaningfully address.

**Standardized preparations and head-to-head comparisons:** The heterogeneity of valerian products used across trials remains the primary barrier to precise meta-analytic conclusions. Future trials should mandate strict standardization criteria (valerenic acid content, extraction method, storage conditions documented) and ideally include pharmacokinetic substudies to verify plasma valerenic acid levels. Head-to-head comparisons of valerian versus low-dose melatonin in chronic insomnia — two of the most commonly used non-pharmaceutical sleep aids — would provide clinically useful comparative efficacy data.

**Objective outcome expansion:** Most trials rely on subjective sleep questionnaires. Future studies should include actigraphy as a minimum objective measure (less expensive than PSG, captures naturalistic sleep in the home environment) with PSG substudies in subgroups. The PSG data from Donath et al. is nearly 25 years old and deserves replication with larger samples and extended durations.

**Specific anxiety disorder populations:** The Miyasaka et al. Cochrane review identified the near-absence of controlled trials in diagnosed anxiety disorders. A well-powered RCT of valerian in DSM-5 generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) — with standardized dosing at 200 mg three times daily and validated anxiety rating scales — would fill a significant evidence gap.

**Menopausal and geriatric populations:** The preliminary evidence from Mirabi and Taavoni suggests meaningful benefits in postmenopausal women. Larger multicenter trials confirming vasomotor and sleep effects in menopause would support valerian as a non-hormonal option. In geriatric populations, where benzodiazepine avoidance is particularly important, a rigorous safety and efficacy trial with fall-risk and cognitive outcomes would be highly valuable.

**Restless legs syndrome:** The Cuellar and Ratcliffe pilot needs replication in a larger sample before RLS can be considered an evidence-supported indication. A trial of 200+ participants with validated RLS diagnostic criteria and both subjective and actigraphic outcome measures would be appropriately powered.

---

## Outlook

Valerian occupies a well-established if somewhat imprecisely quantified position in the evidence base for botanical sleep aids. The meta-analytic evidence for subjective sleep improvement is consistent across multiple independent analyses; the effect on objective sleep architecture is less clear and insufficiently studied. This contrasts with pharmaceutical hypnotics, where polysomnographic effects are well-characterized but dependency and next-day impairment are clinically problematic.

For mild-to-moderate insomnia — particularly sleep onset difficulty in otherwise healthy adults — valerian represents a reasonable first-line or adjunct approach, with the understanding that a 2–4 week trial is necessary to assess full benefit, that dosing above 400 mg is likely necessary, and that drug interactions with sedating medications require careful consideration. The ongoing need for standardized preparations and consistent outcome measures remains the principal methodological gap limiting precise clinical recommendations.
