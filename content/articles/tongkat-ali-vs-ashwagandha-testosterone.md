## TL;DR

**Tongkat ali and ashwagandha both support testosterone, but through completely different mechanisms.** Tongkat ali may directly stimulate testosterone production — studies show +20-30% in some trials. Ashwagandha increases testosterone indirectly by lowering cortisol — high cortisol suppresses testosterone production. Choose based on your primary issue: low energy/libido with normal stress → tongkat ali; high stress/poor sleep + low testosterone → ashwagandha. Evidence for both is moderate at best — neither is a pharmaceutical-grade intervention. **Full comparison below.**

## What Is Tongkat Ali?

Tongkat ali (*Eurycoma longifolia*, also called Longjack or Malaysian ginseng) is a flowering plant native to Southeast Asia. Its root has been used for centuries in traditional medicine as an aphrodisiac and energy tonic. The root contains bioactive compounds including eurycomanone, quassinoids, and alkaloids that are believed to support testosterone production.

The proposed mechanism involves stimulation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis. Specifically, tongkat ali may increase the release of luteinizing hormone (LH) from the pituitary, which signals the testes to produce more testosterone. This is a more direct pathway than ashwagandha's cortisol-mediated approach.

A 2013 study by Talbott et al. randomized 63 moderately stressed adults to tongkat ali (200 mg/day) or placebo for 4 weeks. The tongkat ali group showed significant reductions in cortisol (−16%), tension (−11%), and anger (−12%), with a trend toward increased testosterone. A 2012 study in men with late-onset hypogonadism found that tongkat ali increased total testosterone by 46% over baseline after 1 month.

**Important caveat:** The testosterone data, while promising, comes from small trials (n=30–76) and is not independently replicated at scale. The European Food Safety Authority flagged concerns about potential DNA damage from tongkat ali in 2021, though this was based on in vitro data at high concentrations and the clinical significance is unclear. The EU has restricted tongkat ali in food supplements pending further safety data.

## What Is Ashwagandha?

Ashwagandha (*Withania somnifera*) is covered in detail in our [dedicated guide](/articles/ashwagandha). For this comparison, the key mechanism is: ashwagandha reduces cortisol, and chronically elevated cortisol suppresses testosterone production at multiple levels — including direct inhibition of Leydig cell function and reduced GnRH release from the hypothalamus. By lowering cortisol, ashwagandha removes a brake on testosterone synthesis.

A 2019 study by Lopresti et al. in overweight men aged 40–70 found that 600 mg/day of ashwagandha extract (Shoden) for 8 weeks increased testosterone by 14.7% and DHEA-S by 18% compared to placebo. Cortisol and estrogen were not significantly changed in that particular trial, though other ashwagandha studies have consistently shown cortisol reduction.

## The Cortisol-Testosterone Connection

This is the key concept for choosing between these supplements:

- **Chronic stress → elevated cortisol → reduced testosterone.** Cortisol and testosterone share a precursor (pregnenolone). When the adrenal glands are constantly producing cortisol to manage stress, they "steal" precursor from the testosterone synthesis pathway. Cortisol also directly inhibits testosterone production at the testicular level.
- **This is where ashwagandha excels.** It addresses the root cause (cortisol) rather than the symptom (low testosterone).
- **Tongkat ali targets the downstream effect.** It attempts to stimulate testosterone production directly, regardless of cortisol status.

The practical implication: if your cortisol is normal and testosterone is still low, the problem may not be stress-mediated — making tongkat ali more directly relevant. If you're chronically stressed with elevated cortisol, ashwagandha addresses the underlying physiology.

## Side Effects and Safety

| Concern | Tongkat Ali | Ashwagandha |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Common side effects | Insomnia, restlessness, irritability, headache | Drowsiness, GI upset (usually mild and transient) |
| Hormonal effects | May increase testosterone; theoretically could affect estrogen balance | May affect thyroid function (can increase T3/T4) |
| Liver concerns | Not well-documented | Rare case reports of liver injury; typically resolves on discontinuation |
| Pregnancy | Contraindicated | Contraindicated (may cause uterine contractions) |
| EU regulatory status | Restricted in food supplements (2021) | No restrictions |
| Quality concerns | Significant adulteration risk; sourcing matters | Standardized extracts available (KSM-66, Sensoril, Shoden) |

Tongkat ali has a more concerning safety profile than ashwagandha, primarily due to the 2021 EFSA DNA damage warning (based on in vitro data, but taken seriously by regulators) and the higher risk of adulteration. Ashwagandha's safety concerns are rare (liver injury) and typically associated with specific extracts or high doses.

## How to Decide: A Practical Framework

1. **Test your cortisol.** If it's elevated (high-normal or above), start with ashwagandha. This addresses the root cause.
2. **If cortisol is normal but testosterone is low,** consider tongkat ali — with the understanding that the evidence is thinner and the safety profile is less established.
3. **If both are fine and you're optimizing athletic performance,** either can help — ashwagandha for recovery and strength, tongkat ali for more direct androgen support. Some athletes cycle both.
4. **Get blood work before and after.** Without baseline and follow-up labs, you're guessing. Test total and free testosterone, cortisol, and a basic metabolic panel.
5. **Set a timeline.** Reassess after 6–8 weeks. If you haven't seen meaningful changes by then, the supplement probably isn't working for you.

## Bottom Line

**If your primary issue is stress, poor sleep, or anxiety, and your testosterone is low as a result:** start with ashwagandha. The cortisol-testosterone connection is well-documented, and ashwagandha's cortisol-lowering effects are replicated across multiple trials.

**If your stress is well-managed but you have low libido, energy, or testosterone without an obvious cause:** tongkat ali may be more directly targeted. The evidence is thinner, but the mechanism is more direct.

**If you're not sure:** get your cortisol tested. If it's elevated, start with ashwagandha. If it's normal but testosterone is low, consider tongkat ali. In all cases, sleep, nutrition, and exercise matter more than either supplement.
