# Mitragynine: Evidence, Mechanisms, Safety, and Dependence

**Last Updated:** June 15, 2026

**Important Safety Notice**

Kratom and its alkaloids are not approved by the FDA for any medical use. Serious adverse effects, including dependence, withdrawal, liver injury, seizures, respiratory depression in high-risk contexts, and rare severe outcomes, have been reported.

## Evidence Snapshot

Overall, the human evidence base for mitragynine is **limited to moderate** depending on the outcome. Some signals exist for effects on pain perception and opioid-withdrawal symptoms in observational settings, while data on mood, anxiety, and cognitive outcomes are weaker and more heterogeneous.

Respiratory depression appears less pronounced than with classical full mu-opioid agonists in available studies, but this does not eliminate risk, especially with concentrated extracts, elevated 7-hydroxymitragynine exposure, or polydrug use. Dependence and withdrawal phenomena are documented in preclinical models and human reports, though severity and prevalence require better characterization.

## Pharmacokinetics and Metabolism

Human pharmacokinetic data for mitragynine have improved in recent years but remain incomplete. A randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, dose-escalation study in healthy volunteers examined single and repeated oral doses of encapsulated dried kratom leaf powder [1]. Mitragynine Cmax increased in a generally dose-proportional manner, with median Tmax occurring between about 1.0 and 1.3 hours after single doses and 1.0 to 1.7 hours after multiple daily doses. Terminal half-life estimates reached approximately 43 hours after single doses and longer with repeated administration in some participants. Steady-state concentrations were approached within roughly 8 to 9 days.

A critical feature of mitragynine pharmacokinetics is its conversion to 7-hydroxymitragynine. In vitro studies using human liver microsomes demonstrate that this transformation is mediated primarily by CYP3A4 [2]. In the Huestis et al. human study, measurable plasma concentrations of 7-hydroxymitragynine were observed after oral kratom leaf powder administration. Mean 7-hydroxymitragynine-to-mitragynine concentration ratios ranged from approximately 0.20 to 0.31 after single doses and appeared to decrease slightly, roughly 0.15 to 0.21, with multiple dosing [1].

This metabolic conversion matters because 7-hydroxymitragynine has substantially higher potency and efficacy at the mu-opioid receptor compared with the parent compound. The rate and extent of conversion introduce a possible rate-limiting step. Some preclinical work suggests this metabolic limitation may contribute to a ceiling effect on certain pharmacodynamic outcomes, including respiratory depression, at higher mitragynine exposures [3]. That does **not** imply safety at all doses or in all contexts.

**Key implications**

- Mitragynine is converted to a more potent active metabolite in humans.
- Observed 7-OH formation after leaf powder administration is measurable but appears constrained by metabolic rate in available studies.
- Concentrated or semi-synthetic 7-hydroxymitragynine products create different exposure patterns than traditional leaf material.
- CYP3A4 inhibitors, CYP3A4 inducers, liver function, product composition, and polydrug use are plausible sources of variable effects and adverse outcomes.

## Background and Product Context

*Mitragyna speciosa*, commonly known as [kratom](/compounds/kratom), is a tree native to Southeast Asia. Traditional use of its leaves has been documented in regions including Thailand, Malaysia, and Indonesia, primarily for manual labor support and as a folk remedy. These historical practices involved chewing fresh or dried leaves or preparing them as teas or decoctions.

Modern commercial products range from dried leaf powder to concentrated extracts and isolated or semi-synthetic alkaloids. These formats can create substantially different exposure profiles. Mitragynine is the most abundant indole alkaloid in kratom leaf, typically comprising a major portion of total alkaloid content. Other alkaloids, including 7-hydroxymitragynine, paynantheine, speciogynine, and speciociliatine, are also present.

The relative proportions of these alkaloids vary by plant variety, growing conditions, harvest timing, processing, and manufacturing practices. That variability complicates direct comparisons across studies and commercial products. Concentrated preparations that elevate 7-hydroxymitragynine levels require separate safety interpretation from traditional leaf material.

## Mechanisms of Action

Mitragynine interacts with multiple receptor systems, with opioid receptor activity receiving the most research attention. It acts as a partial agonist at the mu-opioid receptor with lower intrinsic efficacy than morphine and other full agonists. In vitro and animal data also suggest functional selectivity and relatively limited recruitment of the beta-arrestin pathway compared with classical opioids.

Mitragynine also shows activity at other targets, including kappa- and delta-opioid receptors, adrenergic receptors, and serotonin receptors. The clinical relevance of these non-MOR interactions at typical exposure levels remains incompletely characterized.

The metabolic conversion step adds important nuance. Because formation of 7-hydroxymitragynine appears rate-limited in available human pharmacokinetic data, the overall pharmacodynamic profile of mitragynine-dominant leaf preparations may differ from direct exposure to 7-hydroxymitragynine. This distinction becomes especially important when comparing traditional leaf preparations with concentrated products that deliver higher levels of 7-hydroxymitragynine directly.

## Clinical Evidence

Human clinical research on mitragynine remains limited in both quantity and methodological rigor. Most available data come from observational studies and surveys among regular kratom users, with far fewer controlled trials in kratom-naive Western populations.

### Pain and Analgesia-Related Outcomes

Several observational studies and a smaller number of controlled investigations have examined kratom or mitragynine in relation to pain. User surveys frequently report self-perceived reductions in pain. Controlled human data are much more limited, and acute administration studies in healthy volunteers show mixed or modest effects on experimental pain measures. Current human evidence for clinically meaningful, reliable analgesia from mitragynine is **limited**.

### Opioid Withdrawal and Dependence-Related Signals

A recurring theme in the literature is the use of kratom by individuals seeking to manage opioid withdrawal symptoms. Observational reports describe self-reported reductions in withdrawal discomfort. However, the same literature documents that regular kratom use itself can lead to dependence and a withdrawal syndrome upon cessation. Controlled data on mitragynine's ability to attenuate opioid withdrawal in humans are very limited.

For discontinuation support and red-flag symptoms, see the [Kratom 7-OH withdrawal management guide](/guides/kratom-7oh-withdrawal-management). That guide is harm-reduction context, not a self-treatment protocol.

### Mood, Anxiety, and Stress-Related Outcomes

Observational data and user surveys frequently mention improvements in mood or reduced anxiety. Controlled clinical evidence for these effects is weak. Overall, human evidence supporting reliable benefits for mood or anxiety from mitragynine is **limited** and vulnerable to expectancy, selection bias, and confounding.

## Safety and Toxicology

### Respiratory Effects

Available data suggest that mitragynine produces less pronounced respiratory depression than classical full mu-opioid agonists in some experimental models [3, 4]. However, respiratory depression and overdose have still been reported, especially in the context of high exposure, concentrated products, elevated 7-hydroxymitragynine content, or combination with other central nervous system depressants.

"Less pronounced than a full agonist" is not the same as "safe." Individual vulnerability, product composition, tolerance, and co-use with alcohol, benzodiazepines, opioids, sedatives, gabapentinoids, or other depressants can shift the risk profile.

### Dependence and Withdrawal

Both preclinical and human observational data indicate that repeated mitragynine exposure can produce physical dependence. Withdrawal symptoms upon cessation have been described, with features overlapping classical opioid withdrawal. Severity appears to correlate with dose, duration, frequency of use, and product potency.

Dependence liability appears higher with concentrated or high-dose regimens and with products containing elevated 7-hydroxymitragynine. For a compound-level comparison of this risk shift, see [mitragynine vs 7-hydroxymitragynine](/compare/mitragynine-vs-7-hydroxymitragynine).

### Other Serious Adverse Events

Case reports and surveillance data have linked kratom alkaloid use to serious outcomes including liver injury, seizures, cardiovascular effects, and deaths, often with polysubstance exposure or unclear causality. The FDA continues to warn consumers about kratom-related risks and states that there are no FDA-approved kratom drug products or over-the-counter drugs legally marketed in the United States [5].

Concentrated 7-hydroxymitragynine products are a distinct concern. FDA communications have described warning letters, scheduling recommendations for certain products, and enforcement actions involving 7-OH products [6]. Readers should verify current federal, state, and local rules before relying on any legal-status statement.

## Conclusions and Research Gaps

Mitragynine is a pharmacologically complex compound whose effects result from both direct receptor activity and metabolic conversion to the higher-potency agonist 7-hydroxymitragynine. Human clinical data remain limited.

Available data suggest modest and inconsistent signals for certain outcomes alongside clear documentation of dependence liability and withdrawal phenomena. The distinction between traditional leaf material and concentrated or high-7-hydroxymitragynine products is critical for risk assessment.

Major research gaps include larger controlled trials, better long-term safety data, improved product standardization, and stronger understanding of individual factors such as CYP3A4 variability, medication interactions, liver function, and substance-use history.

Until higher-quality evidence is available, conclusions about both potential benefits and risks should remain appropriately cautious.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is mitragynine the same as kratom?**  
No. Mitragynine is one major alkaloid found in kratom leaf. Kratom products contain a wider alkaloid mixture whose composition can vary substantially.

**Is mitragynine the same as 7-hydroxymitragynine?**  
No. Mitragynine can be metabolized into 7-hydroxymitragynine, but 7-OH is generally more potent at the mu-opioid receptor. See the [direct comparison](/compare/mitragynine-vs-7-hydroxymitragynine) for details.

**Is mitragynine FDA-approved?**  
No. There are no FDA-approved kratom drug products or over-the-counter drugs containing kratom legally marketed in the United States.

**Does lower respiratory depression risk mean mitragynine is safe?**  
No. Relative differences versus classical opioids do not eliminate dependence, withdrawal, liver, seizure, cardiovascular, product-quality, or polydrug risks.
