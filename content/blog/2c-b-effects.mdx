2C-B (4-bromo-2,5-dimethoxyphenethylamine) is a synthetic phenethylamine classified as a Schedule I controlled substance in the United States. It has no accepted medical use under federal law and is illegal to manufacture, possess, or distribute in most contexts. This article is an educational reference for experienced researchers and harm-reduction-oriented readers seeking specificity on what is known, what is inferred from related serotonergic compounds, and what remains unknown about its pharmacology and risks.

This is not medical advice, legal advice, a therapeutic recommendation, or an endorsement of illegal drug use. Laws vary by jurisdiction and can change. Anyone facing an exposure, severe anxiety, chest pain, overheating, seizure, loss of consciousness, or suspected serotonin syndrome should seek emergency medical care.

## Safety and Harm Reduction

Safety information comes first. Evidence on 2C-B's acute toxicity is limited. No established human LD50 exists, and the fatal dose remains unknown. High oral doses, especially above commonly reported strong ranges, are associated with intensified negative effects including anxiety, frightening hallucinations, tachycardia, hypertension, and hyperthermia. Rare but serious adverse events, including serotonin syndrome, seizures, and cerebral edema, have been reported in case literature.

The compound is known for a steep dose-response curve: small increases of even 2-5 mg may substantially intensify effects. Commonly reported oral ranges include threshold effects around 5-10 mg, light-to-common experiences around 15-25 mg, and strong effects around 25-40+ mg. These figures come from user reports and observational sources, are not medically endorsed, and should not be treated as a safety guarantee.

Onset is commonly reported within 30-90 minutes, with peak effects around 1-3 hours and total duration around 4-8 hours, plus possible after-effects. Elevated heart rate and blood pressure can occur. Individuals with heart conditions, hypertension, seizure history, or psychiatric vulnerabilities should avoid exposure.

Major interaction risks exist with SSRIs, MAOIs, stimulants, other serotonergics, and alcohol because of possible serotonin toxicity or amplified cardiovascular strain. Cannabis may intensify or prolong effects and can increase anxiety risk.

Regarding cardiac risks: preclinical and receptor-screening evidence indicates that some 2C-series phenethylamines interact with 5-HT2B receptors. 5-HT2B agonism is mechanistically linked to cardiac valvulopathy during chronic or repeated exposure to certain serotonergic drugs, including fenfluramine-related compounds and some ergot derivatives. This does not prove that infrequent 2C-B exposure causes valve disease, but it does mean "no known long-term signal" should not be read as "zero risk." Long-term human data for 2C-B are sparse, and cumulative risk from frequent or high-dose patterns remains poorly characterized.

For comparison, 2C-E is another 2C phenethylamine with overlapping serotonergic pharmacology and published human observational data. Receptorome data and reviews describe 2C-E as active at serotonin receptors including 5-HT2B, but direct head-to-head long-term safety comparisons between 2C-B and 2C-E are limited. Both compounds warrant caution against frequent or high-dose regimens because the relevant cardiac-risk mechanism is most concerning under repeated exposure, and the absence of longitudinal user cohorts leaves a major evidence gap.

Practical harm-reduction steps include using reagent test kits from established organizations such as DanceSafe or Bunk Police to check substance identity before any use, recognizing that reagent testing cannot prove purity or dose and does not make use safe. Other conservative steps include avoiding combinations, avoiding driving or machinery for at least 24 hours, maintaining hydration without overhydration, and having sober support available. Benzodiazepines may be used clinically for severe agitation or panic, but self-medicating adds its own risks and should not replace medical care.

There are no well-established deaths attributed solely to analytically confirmed 2C-B in the reviewed literature, but polydrug use, mislabeling, and variable purity complicate this picture. Absence of evidence is not evidence of safety.

## Reported Effects

User reports and limited clinical observations describe dose-dependent effects that blend mild entactogenic, stimulant, and psychedelic qualities. At lower doses, mood elevation and tactile sensitivity are commonly noted. At higher doses, visual distortions, altered thought patterns, time distortion, nausea, anxiety, and confusion become more prominent.

Effects vary significantly by set, setting, tolerance, individual metabolism, substance identity, and dose accuracy. The same nominal dose can feel very different across people or batches, especially when material is misrepresented or unevenly distributed.

## Pharmacology and Mechanism

2C-B primarily interacts with serotonin receptors. The clearest mechanistic frame is serotonergic signaling across the 5-HT2 receptor family, with 5-HT2A involvement generally associated with psychedelic effects in broader psychedelic research. Some older in vitro models found low efficacy or antagonist-like behavior at 5-HT2A, while later work and receptor-screening literature complicate simple labels such as "agonist" or "antagonist." The conservative summary is that 2C-B has serotonergic activity relevant to perception, mood, and cardiovascular physiology, but the exact human contribution of each receptor subtype remains unsettled.

Additional binding may occur at other serotonin and adrenergic targets, potentially contributing to mild stimulant qualities. Evidence for significant dopaminergic activity is weaker and comes mainly from non-human or indirect models. Metabolism involves monoamine oxidase pathways, which helps explain the special concern around MAOIs and other serotonergic combinations.

Outstanding unknowns include the precise receptor contributions to subjective effects in humans, long-term neuroplastic or neurotoxic potential, cardiac outcomes under repeated use, and pharmacokinetics across diverse populations.

## Takeaway

2C-B produces dose-sensitive psychedelic and entactogenic effects through serotonergic mechanisms, but the evidence base is thin compared with better-studied compounds. The clearest harm-reduction reading is conservative: avoid combinations, recognize the steep response curve, treat reagent testing as a limited identity check rather than a safety seal, and take long-term cardiac uncertainty seriously.

Much remains unknown, particularly around chronic or frequent use. That uncertainty supports caution rather than reassurance.
