# The Rise of Novel Psychoactive Substances in 2026: Trends and Evidence Limitations

<NPSDisclaimer />

**Evidence Snapshot**
Novel psychoactive substances (NPS) continue to emerge across multiple categories. Many of these compounds reach consumers with very limited human safety data. This article provides a high-level overview of current trends, with emphasis on evidence gaps and harm reduction considerations.

**Strong Disclaimer**
This article is for informational and educational purposes only. Many novel psychoactive substances have little to no human clinical safety data. This is not medical advice. These substances can carry serious and poorly characterized risks.

## Introduction

The term "novel psychoactive substances" generally refers to compounds that produce psychoactive effects but are not controlled under existing drug laws in many jurisdictions. These substances often appear in unregulated markets, sometimes under misleading names or as alternatives to traditional drugs.

In recent years, certain categories of NPS have become more prominent, including semi-synthetic opioids related to kratom alkaloids and various synthetic opioid substances. A recurring challenge across many of these compounds is the limited amount of human safety and efficacy data available at the time they reach consumers.

## Current Trends

Several patterns have been observed in the NPS landscape:

- Chemical modification of existing compounds
- Rapid market entry with limited prior safety evaluation
- Limited human research at the time of market appearance
- Product variability and misleading marketing

## Categories of Emerging Substances

Novel psychoactive substances appear across several broad categories, including:

- Kratom-derived compounds (concentrated 7-hydroxymitragynine and semi-synthetic derivatives)
- Synthetic opioids
- Novel benzodiazepine-like substances
- Other stimulant and dissociative compounds

## Evidence Limitations

A central issue across much of the NPS landscape is the significant gap in human research. Many compounds become available before robust clinical studies have been conducted. This creates substantial uncertainty regarding safe dosage, long-term effects, dependence potential, and overdose risk.

## Harm Reduction Considerations

- Many NPS demonstrate higher potency than substances they are compared to or marketed alongside.
- Product composition and dosing can be inconsistent or mislabeled.
- Combining these substances with other central nervous system depressants increases risk substantially.
- There is often limited reliable information regarding dependence and interactions.
- The absence of robust human safety data does not indicate that a substance is low-risk.

## Conclusions

The landscape of novel psychoactive substances continues to evolve. A recurring theme is the limited amount of high-quality human data available for many emerging substances. Significant caution is warranted.
