## Full Article Content

Ashwagandha (*Withania somnifera*) is a traditional Ayurvedic herb classified as an adaptogen. It is commonly studied for its effects on stress, anxiety, cortisol regulation, sleep, and cognitive function. Because stress and sleep difficulties often worsen ADHD symptoms, ashwagandha has attracted interest as a potential supportive option. This article examines the human clinical evidence with priority given to studies in ADHD populations.

### What Ashwagandha Is

Ashwagandha is a small shrub native to India and parts of Africa and the Middle East. The root is the most commonly used part in supplements. It contains withanolides, a group of steroidal lactones believed to contribute to its adaptogenic and anti-inflammatory properties.

Commercial extracts are typically standardized to withanolide content. Two widely studied extracts are KSM-66, a full-spectrum root extract, and Sensoril, a root and leaf extract. These differ in composition and have been tested in different populations and outcomes.

Ashwagandha has a long history of use in traditional medicine for promoting vitality, reducing stress, and supporting sleep. Modern research has focused on its effects on the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis, cortisol levels, and inflammatory markers.

### Why Ashwagandha Matters in ADHD

ADHD is frequently accompanied by elevated stress, anxiety, sleep disturbances, and emotional dysregulation. Chronic stress can exacerbate inattention, impulsivity, and executive function difficulties. Ashwagandha’s primary researched effects — lowering perceived stress and cortisol, improving sleep quality, and supporting emotional regulation — align with common challenges in ADHD.

While ashwagandha does not directly target core dopaminergic or noradrenergic pathways like stimulant medications, its indirect effects on stress physiology and sleep may provide meaningful adjunctive support for some individuals.

### Meta-Analysis and Systematic Review Evidence

A 2024 systematic review examined clinical studies of *Withania somnifera* for ADHD. It identified a small but growing body of research, primarily in pediatric populations, showing signals for improvements in ADHD symptoms, anxiety, and sleep. The review noted methodological limitations across studies, including small sample sizes and varying extract types and durations.

Broader meta-analyses on ashwagandha for stress and anxiety, not limited to ADHD, have generally found significant reductions in perceived stress and anxiety scores compared with placebo. These findings provide context for why ashwagandha may be relevant for ADHD populations, where stress amplification is common.

### RCT Evidence in ADHD Populations

Several randomized controlled trials have evaluated ashwagandha in children with ADHD or attention-related concerns.

One prospective, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled study in children aged 5–12 years with mild ADHD found that ashwagandha root extract significantly improved ADHD rating scale scores compared with placebo after 56 days. Reductions were observed in both inattention and hyperactivity domains.

Another trial in children with ADHD and comorbid anxiety symptoms reported reductions in physiological anxiety, social concerns, and overall anxiety scores with ashwagandha root extract.

A study in healthy children aged 6–12 years with parent-reported attention and memory concerns found improvements in information processing speed, memory tasks, and parent-reported sleep quality after eight weeks of a standardized ashwagandha root extract.

Results across these trials are promising but preliminary. Most studies are relatively small, and larger confirmatory trials are needed. Benefits appear more consistent for stress, anxiety, and sleep-related outcomes than for core inattention in all cases.

### Forms and Extracts

The two most researched extracts are:

- KSM-66: A full-spectrum root extract standardized to withanolides. It has been studied for stress reduction, cognitive support, and sleep. Several ADHD-related trials have used or referenced this extract.
- Sensoril: A root-and-leaf extract with a different withanolide profile. It has shown benefits for stress and cognitive function in various populations.

Other products may use generic ashwagandha root powder or different standardization levels. Root-only extracts are generally preferred in research over leaf-only or combined preparations for consistency.

Choice of extract may influence outcomes. KSM-66 has accumulated more published data on stress, sleep, and cognitive parameters relevant to ADHD.

### Pediatric Evidence

Most direct ADHD research on ashwagandha has been conducted in school-age children. Available RCTs suggest potential benefits for reducing hyperactivity, improving attention scores in some contexts, lowering comorbid anxiety, and supporting sleep quality.

One study specifically in children with mild ADHD reported statistically significant improvements in overall ADHD symptoms after approximately eight weeks. Another focused on anxiety symptoms within an ADHD population showed reductions in multiple anxiety domains.

Evidence quality is currently rated as low to moderate due to study size, duration, and occasional use of co-interventions. Ashwagandha is not a first-line treatment for pediatric ADHD but may offer adjunctive value for stress, anxiety, and sleep when clinically appropriate.

### Adult Evidence

Specific randomized controlled trials of ashwagandha in adults diagnosed with ADHD are limited. Most cognitive and stress-reduction studies have been conducted in healthy adults, stressed populations, or individuals with anxiety or insomnia rather than confirmed ADHD.

General findings in adults show consistent reductions in stress and anxiety scores and improvements in sleep quality with standardized extracts, typically 300–600 mg/day. These effects may be relevant for adults with ADHD who experience prominent stress reactivity or sleep disruption, but direct extrapolation to ADHD symptom management requires caution.

### Dosing and Timing

Doses in published research commonly range from 300 mg to 600 mg per day of standardized root extract, often divided into one or two doses.

Timing considerations:

- Evening or split dosing may support sleep when that is a primary goal.
- Morning dosing is sometimes used when daytime stress or focus support is targeted.
- Effects on stress and sleep are often measured after 6–8 weeks of consistent use.

Individual responses vary. Starting at the lower end of the studied range and monitoring tolerability is a common practical approach. Use in children should always involve clinical supervision.

### Medication Interactions

Ashwagandha may have additive effects with sedating medications, including certain antihistamines, benzodiazepines, or sleep aids. Monitoring for excessive drowsiness is advisable.

It may also influence thyroid hormone levels in some individuals. People with thyroid disorders or those taking thyroid medication should consult a clinician before use.

Ashwagandha has been studied alongside some antidepressant and anxiolytic medications without major reported interactions in short-term trials, but individual variability exists. Anyone taking prescription medications should discuss supplement use with their healthcare provider.

### Who May Benefit Most

Ashwagandha may be most relevant for individuals with ADHD who:

- Experience significant stress, anxiety, or emotional dysregulation that worsens core symptoms.
- Have prominent sleep difficulties.
- Are already using evidence-based ADHD treatments and seeking adjunctive support for stress or sleep under clinical guidance.
- Prefer herbal adaptogenic options with a growing but still emerging evidence base.

It is not positioned as a replacement for behavioral interventions or medication when clinically indicated.

### Who Should Avoid It or Use Extra Caution

Caution or avoidance is warranted in:

- Individuals with autoimmune conditions, due to theoretical immune-modulating effects.
- People with hyperthyroidism or those on thyroid medication, because ashwagandha may affect thyroid levels.
- Pregnant or breastfeeding individuals, due to insufficient safety data.
- Those taking multiple sedating medications or with significant liver or kidney impairment without medical supervision.

New or concerning symptoms, such as excessive sedation, gastrointestinal upset, or changes in energy or mood, should prompt discontinuation and medical evaluation.

### Tracking Response

Systematic tracking supports informed decisions:

- Daily or weekly ratings of stress, anxiety, focus, emotional regulation, and sleep quality.
- Sleep metrics, including onset, awakenings, and refreshment.
- Any side effects.
- Notes on timing and dose.

Review trends after 6–8 weeks of consistent use. If target symptoms show meaningful improvement, continued use may be reasonable with periodic reassessment. If no clear benefit is observed, discontinuation is appropriate.

### Research Gaps

Current evidence has important limitations:

- Most ADHD-specific trials are small and relatively short.
- Few large, long-term monotherapy studies exist.
- Adult ADHD data are particularly sparse.
- Direct head-to-head comparisons of different extracts in ADHD populations are lacking.
- Optimal dosing and duration for specific symptom clusters remain incompletely defined.

Larger, well-controlled trials in both children and adults with ADHD would strengthen clinical guidance.

### Conclusion

Ashwagandha shows promising signals for reducing stress, anxiety, and sleep difficulties in children with ADHD, with some studies also reporting improvements in overall symptom scores. Evidence quality is currently low to moderate, and benefits appear most relevant as adjunctive support rather than primary treatment.

Standardized root extracts, particularly KSM-66, have the most published data. Ashwagandha is generally well tolerated at studied doses but is not suitable for everyone. Clinical supervision is recommended, especially for children or when other medications are in use.

Expectations should remain modest and evidence-based. Systematic tracking helps determine individual value. Ashwagandha may be a reasonable option to discuss with a clinician when stress, anxiety, or sleep are prominent concerns within an ADHD presentation.

## FAQ

### Does ashwagandha help with ADHD?

Some randomized trials in children show improvements in ADHD symptom scores, hyperactivity, anxiety, and sleep. Evidence is promising but still preliminary and not sufficient to recommend it as a primary treatment.

### Which form of ashwagandha is best studied for ADHD?

KSM-66, a standardized full-spectrum root extract, has been used in several relevant studies. Sensoril, a root and leaf extract, has also been researched but less specifically in ADHD populations.

### How much ashwagandha should I take for ADHD support?

Studied doses typically range from 300–600 mg per day of standardized root extract. Individual needs vary. Clinical guidance is recommended for children.

### How long does it take for ashwagandha to work?

Stress and sleep effects are often measured after 6–8 weeks of consistent use in research. Some individuals report earlier changes in relaxation or sleep onset.

### Can ashwagandha replace ADHD medication?

No. Ashwagandha has not demonstrated comparable effect sizes to standard treatments for core ADHD symptoms. It may serve as complementary support for stress or sleep in appropriate cases.

### Is ashwagandha safe for children with ADHD?

Several trials have used it in school-age children with generally good tolerability. Use should be supervised by a qualified clinician.

### Does ashwagandha interact with ADHD medications?

Known interactions appear limited, but additive sedative effects are possible with certain medications. Always consult the prescribing clinician.

### What are common side effects of ashwagandha?

Most people tolerate it well. Reported side effects can include gastrointestinal discomfort, drowsiness, or rarely changes in thyroid function or hormone levels.

### Should adults with ADHD take ashwagandha?

Adult-specific ADHD trials are limited. General evidence supports benefits for stress and sleep in adults, which may be relevant for some with ADHD. Tracking response is important.

### How do I know if ashwagandha is helping?

Track stress, anxiety, focus, emotional regulation, and sleep metrics over 6–8 weeks. Compare periods with and without the supplement when appropriate and discuss results with a clinician.
