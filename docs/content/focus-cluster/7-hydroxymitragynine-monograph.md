# 7-Hydroxymitragynine (7-OH): Benefits, Risks, Safety & Research Review (2026)

**Last Updated:** June 14, 2026

**Important Safety Notice**  
This content is for educational purposes only and does **not** constitute medical advice. 7-Hydroxymitragynine (7-OH), especially in concentrated forms, carries risks of dependence, withdrawal, and respiratory depression. No FDA-approved drugs contain 7-OH. Concentrated 7-OH products are not lawful as dietary supplements or conventional foods. Always consult a qualified healthcare professional before considering any substance.

---

## Quick Answer: What Is 7-Hydroxymitragynine?

7-Hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) is a naturally occurring kratom alkaloid and a metabolite of mitragynine. Although it exists in trace amounts in traditional kratom leaf, some modern commercial products contain concentrated levels substantially higher than those found in nature. Current evidence suggests that concentrated 7-OH products have a different risk profile than traditional kratom preparations, with concerns including dependence, withdrawal, and respiratory depression.

---

## What We Know and What We Don’t Know

**What We Know**  
- 7-OH is a potent partial agonist at the mu-opioid receptor with significantly higher affinity and efficacy than mitragynine in most in vitro assays [1].  
- It is formed as a metabolite of mitragynine via CYP3A4 in humans [2].  
- Concentrated 7-OH products have been associated with serious adverse events in post-market surveillance [3].  
- The FDA recommended scheduling certain 7-OH products in July 2025 and has taken enforcement action against some manufacturers [4, 5].  
- Traditional kratom leaf contains only trace amounts of 7-OH as part of a complex alkaloid matrix [6].

**What We Don’t Know**  
- There are no large, well-controlled human clinical trials evaluating the efficacy or safety of *isolated* 7-OH.  
- The contribution of 7-OH to the effects of whole kratom leaf in humans remains incompletely characterized.  
- Long-term safety data for concentrated 7-OH products are limited.  
- Individual variability in response due to genetics, tolerance, and metabolism is high and poorly quantified.

---

**Not a Supplement. Not FDA-Approved.**  
7-Hydroxymitragynine is **not** recognized as a dietary supplement ingredient. Concentrated 7-OH products have been deemed adulterated by the FDA. They have no approved medical use. Products containing concentrated 7-OH should not be assumed to be safe or legal simply because they are sold commercially.

## Introduction

7-Hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) is a minor alkaloid found in the kratom plant (*Mitragyna speciosa*) and a metabolite of its primary alkaloid, mitragynine. In recent years, commercial products containing significantly elevated levels of 7-OH have appeared on the market. These concentrated products differ substantially from traditional whole-leaf kratom preparations in both composition and risk profile.

In July 2025, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration recommended that certain 7-OH products be placed under the Controlled Substances Act and issued warning letters to companies marketing them [4]. Enforcement actions, including product seizures, have followed [5]. These developments have highlighted the need for clear, evidence-based information distinguishing natural kratom leaf from concentrated 7-OH products.

This monograph reviews the available scientific evidence on 7-OH, including its pharmacology, human pharmacokinetic data, safety signals, and regulatory status as of June 2026. It is intended as an educational resource only.

## Background and Alkaloid Context

The kratom plant contains more than 50 alkaloids [6]. Mitragynine is the most abundant, typically comprising 60–70% of total alkaloid content in dried leaf [6]. 7-Hydroxymitragynine is normally present only in trace amounts.

In the mid-2020s, products with greatly elevated 7-OH content became increasingly visible in the U.S. market. These are often produced through enrichment or semi-synthesis and contain 7-OH levels far exceeding those found in natural leaf. Regulatory authorities have noted that such concentrated products are pharmacologically and toxicologically distinct from traditional kratom leaf preparations.

---

## Chemistry and Receptor Pharmacology

7-Hydroxymitragynine is structurally related to mitragynine, differing by a hydroxyl group at the 7-position. This modification results in substantially higher binding affinity at the human μ-opioid receptor (MOR). Reported Ki values for 7-OH at MOR typically range from approximately 7–70 nM, compared with significantly higher, weaker-affinity values for mitragynine [1].

In functional assays, 7-OH acts as a partial agonist at MOR with higher efficacy than mitragynine in several systems. Preclinical studies in rodents have shown that 7-OH produces dose-dependent antinociceptive effects. However, it also produces classic opioid-like effects, including respiratory depression, tolerance, physical dependence, and rewarding properties [8].

7-OH is formed from mitragynine primarily through CYP3A4-mediated oxidation in human liver microsomes and in vivo [2].

---

## Human Pharmacokinetic Data

The most comprehensive human pharmacokinetic study of mitragynine and 7-OH to date is Huestis et al. (2024) [2]. In this randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled dose-escalation trial, participants received oral encapsulated dried kratom leaf powder.

Key findings included rapid absorption, dose-proportional increases in plasma concentration, and steady-state achievement within 7–9 days. These data provide important context for interpreting exposure after oral administration of whole kratom leaf. No dedicated pharmacokinetic studies of isolated or purified 7-OH in humans have been published.

## Clinical Evidence and Quality of Evidence in Humans

Dedicated randomized controlled trials evaluating the therapeutic effects or safety of *isolated* 7-OH in humans are not available. Most human data derive from studies of whole kratom leaf preparations.

## Safety and Adverse Event Profile

Preclinical data indicate that 7-OH can produce respiratory depression, tolerance, and physical dependence [8]. Human safety data for isolated 7-OH are limited.

Post-market reports and regulatory actions have documented serious adverse events, including cases involving respiratory depression and dependence, associated with concentrated 7-OH products. Many such reports involve polysubstance use.

Individuals with respiratory conditions, a history of substance use disorder, or those taking other central nervous system depressants may be at elevated risk.

## Regulatory Status (June 2026)

As of this review, the FDA had recommended scheduling certain 7-OH products, while federal scheduling status remained subject to DEA review. Readers should verify current federal, state, and local law before relying on any regulatory status statement.

State-level rules for kratom and concentrated 7-OH products vary and may change quickly. Readers should check current state and local law before relying on any legal-status statement.

Concentrated 7-OH products are considered adulterated and are not lawful as dietary supplements or conventional foods under current FDA policy.

## Alkaloid Interactions and the Whole-Leaf Matrix

Kratom contains multiple alkaloids beyond mitragynine and 7-OH. Minor alkaloids such as paynantheine and speciogynine exhibit different receptor binding profiles. Human pharmacokinetic studies confirm that 7-OH formation from mitragynine can be altered by CYP3A4 inhibitors [2]. Whole-leaf preparations deliver 7-OH within a complex matrix, whereas concentrated products deliver it directly at higher levels.

---

## Conclusions

7-Hydroxymitragynine is a potent mu-opioid receptor partial agonist formed as a metabolite of mitragynine. Preclinical data demonstrate opioid-like activity with associated risks. Human clinical data on isolated 7-OH are very limited. Concentrated 7-OH products differ materially from traditional whole-leaf kratom in both composition and observed safety signals.

At present, there is insufficient high-quality human evidence to support therapeutic use of isolated 7-OH. Concentrated products carry documented risks and are subject to regulatory scrutiny.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is 7-OH the same as kratom?**  
No. 7-OH is one specific alkaloid that occurs in trace amounts in natural kratom leaf and is also formed as a metabolite. Concentrated 7-OH products contain much higher levels and are distinct from traditional leaf preparations.

**Are there human clinical trials on pure 7-OH?**  
No large, well-controlled clinical trials evaluating isolated 7-OH for efficacy or safety have been published as of June 2026.

**Why has the FDA taken action on 7-OH products?**  
The FDA has cited concerns about safety, including risks of dependence and respiratory depression, and has determined that concentrated 7-OH products are not lawful as dietary supplements.

**Is traditional kratom leaf the same as concentrated 7-OH products?**  
No. Traditional preparations contain 7-OH only in trace amounts as part of a complex alkaloid matrix. Concentrated products deliver much higher levels of 7-OH directly.
